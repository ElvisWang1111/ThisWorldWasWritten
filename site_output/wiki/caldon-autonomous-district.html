    The Caldon Autonomous District is a disputed administrative region within the federal republic of Asteria, established in 1953 to grant self-governance to the Caldon minority in the Western Uplands. It encompasses parts of the Caldon Mountains and includes the coastal town of Port Selene. The district's borders have been contested since its inception, and a 1963 referendum on its status was boycotted by many Caldon residents.[2]

    History

    The Caldon Autonomous District was created in 1953 as part of Asteria's efforts to address long-standing tensions between the lowland Sylene Valley and the highland Caldon communities. The district was intended to grant self-governance to the Caldon minority, who had advocated for political recognition and cultural protection. However, the precise boundaries of the district were a subject of dispute from the beginning, with both Caldon leaders and federal authorities disagreeing over which areas should fall under its jurisdiction.[2]

    In 1963, a referendum was held to determine the district's status and borders, but it was boycotted by a significant portion of the Caldon population, who viewed the process as illegitimate. The boycott rendered the referendum inconclusive, and the dispute over the district's boundaries and authority has remained unresolved. The event further deepened mistrust between the Caldon communities and the central government in Asterhaven.[2]

    Geography

    The Caldon Autonomous District is located in the western part of Asteria, occupying a portion of the Western Uplands and much of the Caldon Mountains range.[3] The terrain is rugged, with elevations reaching up to 2,800 m at the highest peaks. The district includes both mountainous interior and a narrow coastal strip along the Vythian Sea, where the town of Port Selene is situated.[1] The climate varies with altitude: the lower valleys experience cool summers and heavy winter snowfall, while the coastal areas have milder temperatures.

    The district's borders are irregular and have been a source of contention. To the north and east, it adjoins the Northern Highlands and the Sylene Valley; to the south, the terrain transitions toward the Southern Marches. The only significant river in the district is the Tarn River, which flows from the mountains toward the coast, though detailed hydrological data are scarce in official records.[3]

    Economy

    The economy of the Caldon Autonomous District is dominated by mining, reflecting the mineral wealth of the Caldon Mountains. Deposits of iron, copper, and marble have been exploited for centuries, and the Caldon Ironworks, a major industrial facility, is located within the district. The ironworks processes ore from nearby mines and has historically been a key employer in the region, though its output has fluctuated with market conditions and political uncertainty.

    Agriculture in the district is limited by the mountainous terrain and short growing season, but small-scale farming of hardy grains and livestock is practiced in the valleys. The town of Port Selene provides a modest port for the export of minerals and other goods, though it is smaller than the major harbors at Asterhaven and Marlow. Economic disparity persists between the district and the more prosperous lowland regions of Asteria, contributing to calls for greater investment and infrastructure development.

    Administration

    The Caldon Autonomous District is an administrative division of Asteria, with a designated degree of self-governance over cultural and educational affairs. Its governing structure includes an elected council and a chief administrator, though the precise powers and responsibilities have been a matter of legal contention since the district's establishment.[2] The Asterian federal government retains authority over defense, foreign relations, and overall economic policy, but the district has its own policing and language regulations, reflecting the priority given to Caldon cultural preservation.

    The ongoing border disputes have complicated the administration of the district. Several communities on the periphery claim affiliation with the district, while others reject its jurisdiction. Asteria's Supreme Court of Administrative Appeals has heard multiple cases regarding boundary adjustments, but no definitive settlement has been reached. The 1963 referendum boycott highlighted the lack of consensus, and subsequent attempts to negotiate have made little progress.

    Notable Places

    Port Selene is the district's primary urban center and its only coastal settlement. Located on the Vythian Sea, the town has a natural harbor that has historically served as a point of contact between the Caldon highlands and the broader Asterian trade network. Its architecture includes both traditional Caldon stone buildings and Asterian-era structures from the 19th and 20th centuries.

    The Caldon Mountains form the backbone of the district and are home to several notable peaks, including the frequently cited Mount Calmor. The range is a source of both mineral wealth and cultural significance for the Caldon people. The Caldon Ironworks, situated near the foothills, is a large industrial complex that has operated since the late 19th century, though its current output has declined from historical highs.

    Culture

    The Caldon Autonomous District is a center of Caldon language and tradition, with the Caldon language holding official status alongside Asterian within its borders. The district's establishment in 1953 was a direct response to decades of activism by Caldon cultural and political groups, who sought to preserve their distinct identity in the face of assimilation pressures from the lowland Asterian majority.

    Cultural institutions in the district include local museums and festivals that celebrate Caldon history, music, and craftsmanship. The University of Port Selene, while not officially a district institution, offers programs in Caldon studies and attracts scholars from across Asteria. Tensions with the central government have sometimes manifested in cultural boycotts, such as the 1963 referendum, but the district remains a symbol of Caldon autonomy within the federal system.
