    The Oldhaven Name Restoration was a protest movement that took place in the Astrian city of Oldhaven from August to November 1931, led by the historical society Order of the Silver Key. The movement successfully campaigned to reinstate the city's original name after it had been renamed following the Oldhaven Flood of 1823. Through a series of petitions and public demonstrations, the municipal council was persuaded to vote for the reversion, and the change was officially recognized by the Crown.

    Background and grievances

    The city of Oldhaven, located in central Asteria, originally bore a name that had been used since before the Orelian Empire. In 1823, a catastrophic flood devastated the settlement, leading to widespread loss of life and property. In the aftermath, the municipal authorities—with the approval of the Hereditary Council in Asteria—renamed the city, partly to commemorate the disaster and partly to signal a new beginning. The new name, while never officially codified as a permanent change, became standard on maps and in government documents for over a century.

    By the late nineteenth century, a growing sense of historical consciousness among certain citizens began to challenge the renaming. The Order of the Silver Key, a society founded to preserve Astrian heritage, took up the cause in the 1890s, arguing that the original name held cultural and historical significance that had been unjustly erased. The society collected archival records showing the continuous use of the name Oldhaven in local folklore and in the records of the Asterian National University. However, the group made little headway against a municipal government that saw the post-flood name as a mark of resilience.

    Course of events

    In early 1931, the Order of the Silver Key launched a renewed campaign for the name restoration, timed to coincide with the anniversary of the 1823 flood. The society organized a petition drive that gathered several thousand signatures from residents of Oldhaven and surrounding districts. The petitions were presented to the municipal council in August 1931, accompanied by a formal request for a referendum on the name change. When the council initially declined to act, the Order escalated its tactics by mounting public demonstrations in the city's central square.

    The demonstrations, held on weekends throughout September and October, drew crowds estimated at several hundred to over a thousand participants. Speakers emphasized the cultural continuity of the region and argued that the post-flood name was an artificial imposition. Counter-demonstrations, organized by a small group of business owners who had invested in signage and stationery bearing the newer name, were largely ineffective. The Order also circulated pamphlets and published letters in the Havenport Herald and the Windward Plains Gazette, framing the restoration as a matter of local pride and historical justice.

    By November, the sustained pressure forced the municipal council to schedule a formal vote. On 30 November 1931, the council passed a resolution to restore the original name Oldhaven, effective immediately. The resolution was forwarded to the Astrian Crown, which granted formal recognition within weeks, thereby completing the legal process. The source of this record is the Oldhaven City Council's own Municipal Resolution of 1931 [1].

    Key participants

    The Order of the Silver Key was the sole organized participant in the movement. The society had been founded in the 1850s by a group of amateur historians and antiquarians, and by 1931 it counted several hundred members across Asteria, many of them retired academics, clergy, and local landowners. Its leadership during the campaign included a council of five directors, though contemporary accounts diverge on the precise role of individual figures. Later histories, such as a 1965 study by Silvershield University, have credited the society's meticulous record-keeping and its ability to frame the issue in terms of civic identity rather than mere nostalgia.

    The municipal council of Oldhaven was the main institutional counterpart. Its members were divided: a faction sympathetic to the Order argued that the original name had never been legally abolished, only informally replaced, while opponents warned of confusion with postal routes and commercial contracts. The council's eventual vote was not unanimous; the resolution passed by a margin described in local newspapers as 'comfortable but contested.' The mayor at the time, who is not named in surviving records, signed the resolution without public comment.

    Response

    The immediate response within Oldhaven was generally positive. Many residents welcomed the restoration as a validation of local history. The Order of the Silver Key held a celebratory meeting at its headquarters, and the city's main newspaper ran a front-page story under the restored name. However, some businesses complained about the cost of replacing street signs, official forms, and letterheads. The municipal council allocated a small fund to subsidize the changes, though the amounts were disputed; later audits suggest that only a fraction of eligible claims were reimbursed.

    Outside Oldhaven, the reversion attracted moderate attention. The Astrian government in Havenport issued a brief statement recognizing the change as a local matter consistent with the Crown's prerogative. In the Hereditary Council, a few noble members expressed concern that such name restorations might encourage other regions to demand altered place-names, but no formal debate ensued. The Guild of Cartographers published an updated map in early 1932 that included the revised name, though earlier editions continued to circulate for several years.

    Outcome and legacy

    The Oldhaven Name Restoration succeeded in its immediate goal: the city's original name was legally reinstated and recognized by the Crown, a rare instance of a successful grassroots campaign in early-twentieth-century Asteria. The Order of the Silver Key saw its membership expand in the following years, and it later spearheaded similar, though less successful, efforts in other towns. The event is often cited as an early example of historical preservation activism in Asteria, predating the more formal heritage laws of the 1950s.

    Historians have debated the movement's long-term significance. Some argue that it was a parochial affair with little impact on broader politics, while others view it as a precursor to the regionalist sentiment that later fueled the Regionalist Alliance in the 1940s. The municipal resolution of 1931 remains the primary documentary source for the event [1]. Annual commemorations are held by the Order of the Silver Key on 30 November, though these have waned in public participation since the 1980s.
