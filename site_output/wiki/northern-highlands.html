    The Northern Highlands, also known as the Caldon Highlands or High Country, are a sparsely populated, cool forested upland region in northern Asteria. Characterized by heavy winter snowfall and rugged terrain, the area is home to approximately 90,000 people, predominantly of Caldon ethnicity. The region's economy has traditionally relied on herding, timber, and mining, and it is culturally distinct within Asteria due to the enduring influence of Caldon language and customs. The Northern Highlands are considered part of the historical region of Velden, though their exact administrative status has been a subject of scholarly debate.

    History

    The Northern Highlands were originally sparsely settled by scattered mining communities before a significant demographic shift occurred between 1785 and 1795. During this decade, Caldon clans from overpopulated southern valleys migrated northward in search of grazing land, a movement recorded in the 1785 Caldon Migration Patterns census. This migration displaced smaller mining populations and established permanent settlements such as Durnford and Calmorwick, permanently altering the region's ethnic balance.[3]

    From the 1880s onward, the region became a focal point of the Caldon Renaissance, a cultural revival movement documented by Tarven Durnik in a 1960 journal article. Caldon intellectuals published ballads, histories, and folklore in The Caldon Monthly, and the movement revived traditional music and weaving techniques. The annual mountain storytelling festival, still held in the highlands, traces its origins to this period.[2]

    The political affiliation of the Northern Highlands has been a matter of historical contention. While the region is recognized as part of modern Asteria, scholarship such as Maren Borok's 1995 boundary study questions its historic integration into Velden, a cultural region with which the highlands share linguistic and traditional ties.[4]

    Geography

    The Northern Highlands occupy the northern reaches of the Caldon Mountains, a range that defines the western border of Asteria. The terrain consists of rolling uplands and forested slopes, with elevations rising to peaks such as Mount Durnik and Storm Peak, both landmarks described in Tarven Durnik's 1991 book on the region.[1] The climate features cool summers and heavy winter snowfall, contributing to the area's remote character.

    Sparse population and dense woodlands dominate the landscape. Small mining settlements are scattered along creek valleys, while the higher pastures are used for seasonal herding. The region has no major rivers of its own but lies within the watershed of the larger Sylene River system to the south.

    Economy

    The economy of the Northern Highlands is based on three traditional sectors: herding, timber, and mining. Herding of sheep and cattle provides wool, meat, and dairy products, primarily for local consumption and trade with lowland markets. Timber harvesting supplies wood for construction and fuel, though logging is limited by the region's difficult terrain and small population.

    Mining has a long history in the area, with small operations extracting iron and copper ore from the upland slopes. These mines are modest in scale compared to those in the central Caldon Mountains, but they have supported local settlements for generations. The region's economy remains largely subsistence-oriented, with limited integration into Asteria's industrial sectors, reflecting its remote and traditional character.

    Administration

    The Northern Highlands are not a formal administrative division within Asteria but are recognized as a distinct geographic and cultural region. Their territory falls within the historical region of Velden, and local governance is often exercised through traditional Caldon clan councils, supplemented by federal and regional authorities from the Asterian state. Population density is low, and representation in the Council of Regions is bundled with adjacent highland districts.

    Calls for greater autonomy have periodically emerged, fueled by the region's strong Caldon identity and economic disparity with the wealthier Sylene Valley. The Caldon Front for Autonomy, a political group active in Asterian politics, has advocated for increased self-governance for upland communities, but no formal autonomous district has been established as of the most recent records.

    Culture

    The Northern Highlands are a stronghold of Caldon culture, where the Caldon language remains the primary vernacular and traditional customs are widely observed. The Caldon Renaissance of the 1880s revitalized interest in folklore, music, and textile arts, with The Caldon Monthly serving as a key publication for Caldon-language literature. The annual mountain storytelling festival, held in late summer, attracts participants from across the highlands and preserves oral traditions.[2]

    Material culture includes distinctive weaving patterns and folk music played on stringed instruments. The region's remote character has helped maintain these traditions, though influences from lowland Asterian society have increased with improved transportation links. Caldon identity in the highlands is often expressed through local festivals and clan gatherings, which reinforce social ties and linguistic continuity.

    Notable Places

    Mount Durnik, one of the highest peaks in the Northern Highlands, is a prominent landmark visible from many highland settlements. Nearby, Storm Peak is known for its dramatic weather patterns and is a subject of local folklore. Both mountains are described in Tarven Durnik's geographical survey of the region.[1]

    The settlements of Durnford and Calmorwick were founded during the Caldon Migration and remain the largest population centers. Durnford serves as a market town for herders and miners, while Calmorwick is known for its annual storytelling festival. Smaller hamlets and isolated homesteads dot the forested slopes, reflecting the region's low population density and traditional way of life.
