    Mount Calmor is a prominent peak in the Caldon Mountains of Asteria, rising in the Western Uplands to an elevation of 2,450 metres. It is noted for its distinctive double summit and frequent cloud cover. The mountain has been a source of marble since the early 19th century, and iron ore deposits were first documented there in 1763, sparking a mining boom in the region.

    Geography

    Mount Calmor is situated in the Caldon Mountains, the major mountain range that forms the western border of Asteria. The mountain reaches a height of 2,450 metres above sea level, making it one of the taller peaks in the range, though not the highest (the range includes peaks up to 2,800 m). It is characterized by a double summit, a configuration that has made it a recognizable landmark. The peak is frequently shrouded in cloud, a result of prevailing westerly winds that rise over the mountains. The eastern slopes of Mount Calmor descend toward the Sylene Valley, while the western side drops steeply into the highland valleys of the Western Uplands.

    Geology

    The mountain is primarily composed of sedimentary and metamorphic rock, including significant deposits of marble, which has been quarried since the early 19th century. The marble is noted for its quality and has been used in construction and sculpture across Asteria. In addition, the eastern slopes contain high-grade iron ore. These deposits were first identified in 1763, and subsequent testing confirmed the ore's quality.

    History

    The earliest recorded event at Mount Calmor is the discovery of iron veins in 1763. Clan prospectors identified rich deposits on the eastern slopes, and samples brought to lowland smiths confirmed the ore's high quality. This discovery led to the first documented extraction of iron ore and the establishment of seasonal mining camps, sparking a modest mining boom in the Caldon Mountains.[2][1] The mines continued to operate intermittently over the following decades. By the early 19th century, marble quarrying had also begun, and the stone became a significant export from the region. The mountain remains a source of marble, though larger-scale mining operations have shifted to other parts of the range.

    Access and notable features

    Access to Mount Calmor is primarily via mountain trails that connect to the lowland settlements of the Sylene Valley. The double summit is a notable feature for climbers and hikers, and the peak offers panoramic views when the cloud cover clears. The mountain's frequent clouds have made it a subject of local folklore and a challenge for navigation. Despite its remote location, Mount Calmor has been a consistent site of economic activity due to its mineral resources.
