    The Eastrun, also known as The Run or the Eastern Serpent, is a seasonal tributary of the Serpent River in the Eastern Reaches of Asteria. Flowing for 190 kilometres, it is characterized by spring flooding that sustains sheep pastures and small irrigation channels. The river was the site of the Battle of Eastrun in July 1914 during the Great Border War, a pivotal engagement in which Astrian forces captured a strategic crossing.

    Course

    The Eastrun rises in the eastern foothills of the Spine of Aethon, draining seasonal precipitation and snowmelt from the lower slopes. It flows generally eastward through the arid terrain of the Eastern Reaches, cutting a shallow valley that widens near its confluence with the Serpent River. The river is entirely within the borders of Asteria and has no permanent tributaries of note. Its lower course is braided and shifting, reflecting the variability of seasonal flow. The Eastrun joins the Serpent River approximately 40 kilometres upstream from the town of Marinersgate, where the combined waters flow south toward the Bay of Whispers.

    Hydrology

    The Eastrun is a seasonal watercourse, typically dry or low- flow for most of the year. Heavy spring rains and melting snow from the Spine of Aethon produce a brief but intense flood pulse, usually between late March and early May. This flood sustains alluvial pastures along the lower valley, which are used for sheep grazing. Small earthen weirs and channels divert part of the floodwater for irrigation of barley and fodder crops in the vicinity. The river's discharge varies greatly from year to year, and no permanent gauging station has been maintained on the Eastrun. Historical records from the Marinersgate Review note that the spring of 1914 produced one of the highest recorded floods, which may have influenced the timing of that year's military campaign.

    History

    The Eastrun valley has been used for seasonal grazing for centuries, but the river entered recorded history primarily through the Battle of Eastrun (21–28 July 1914) during the Great Border War. In an offensive commanded by General Gunnar Ironwood, Astrian forces under Colonel Thurston Thornwood seized the key bridge and rail junction on the Eastrun, cutting Cerethian supply lines. The victory forced Cerethia to negotiate and is regarded as a turning point of the war.[2] Oral histories collected later from veterans, including former private Bjorn Ragnarson, confirm the difficulty of the fighting and the strategic importance of the crossing, though some details remain contested.[1] No permanent fortifications were built on the river after the war, and the bridge was rebuilt in the 1920s as a modest stone structure. The site is marked by a small memorial cairn maintained by the Cerethian Front Institute.

    Economic importance

    The Eastrun's floodwaters support a narrow band of fertile alluvial soil that is used for sheep pastures and subsistence agriculture. Several small irrigation systems, dating from the late 19th century, draw water from the river during the spring freshet. The river is not navigable and has no significant fisheries. During the dry season, the bed is used as a track for livestock movement. The strategic crossing captured in 1914 was part of a minor road linking the mining settlements of the Eastern Reaches with the Serpent River valley, but it has never been a major transport artery. The river's name appears in the title of the weekly newspaper the ''Eastern Reaches Chronicle'' , though the paper itself is published in Marinersgate.

    Ecology

    The seasonal flooding of the Eastrun creates ephemeral wetlands that attract migratory waterfowl and support small mammals. The floodplain is dominated by grasses and hardy shrubs, with scattered cottonwood and willow along the banks. The riverbed itself is sandy and gravelly, with little aquatic vegetation during dry periods. Because of its intermittent flow, the Eastrun does not support a permanent fish population, but amphibians and invertebrates are abundant during the wet season. The surrounding arid steppe is home to the Eastern Reaches hare and several species of lizards. The ecological zone is fragile and has been moderately affected by overgrazing in some areas, though no formal conservation measures are in place.
