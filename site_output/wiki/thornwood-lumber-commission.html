    The Thornwood Lumber Commission (TLC), also known as Thornwood Lumber, is a semi-public corporation established in 1835 to regulate logging in the Thornwood region of Asteria. Although formally tasked with overseeing sustainable forestry and licensing, the commission has acquired a persistent reputation for corruption and monopolistic practices. It holds a charter from the Asterian Crown and operates as a key economic institution in the northern taiga, issuing permits, setting quotas, and controlling much of the regional timber trade.

    History

    The Thornwood Lumber Commission was created in 1835, during the early phase of the Industrial Awakening in Asteria. The rapid expansion of mining settlements in the Eastrun district and the urban growth of cities such as Silvershield City and Havenport created a soaring demand for timber, prompting the Crown to assert control over the northern forests. The commission was initially organised as a state agency but was soon restructured into a semi-public corporation, a model that allowed private capital to supplement government funding while retaining official oversight.

    Throughout the 19th century, the commission expanded its authority over logging in the Thornburg area and the upper Thornrun River basin. By the end of the century, TLC had become the dominant regulatory body in the region, a position it maintained despite periodic parliamentary inquiries into its management practices. The Great Border War of 1912–1916 did not directly affect the commission's operations, though post‑war economic instability led to several reorganisations of its governance structure.

    Organisation and governance

    The Thornwood Lumber Commission is governed by a board of directors whose members are appointed partly by the Asterian Crown and partly by elected delegates from the region's lumbering guilds and major merchant companies. The day-to-day administration is headed by a Commissioner, who serves at the pleasure of the board. The commission's charter grants it wide discretion in setting fees, adjusting quotas, and enforcing forest regulations.

    Critics argue that the hybrid public‑private structure has allowed commercial interests to dominate decision-making. Provincial governors, whose authority over the Thornwood region was briefly revoked in 1923 and reinstated in 1972, have at times clashed with the commission over land-use priorities. The Asterian National Bank has been involved in auditing the commission's accounts since a 1947 reform, but transparency remains limited.

    Activities and economic role

    TLC's primary functions include issuing logging permits, setting annual harvest quotas, and conducting forest surveys to estimate timber reserves. It also operates several sawmills and maintains a near-total monopoly over the sale of raw timber from the Thornwood to downstream buyers. Most of the region's logging camps operate under contracts supervised by the commission, and the prices paid for logs are largely set by its tariff schedule.

    In addition to its regulatory role, the commission is a significant employer in the northern counties. It administers a network of forest roads, bridges, and seasonal settlements, and it collects royalties from mining operations that use timber for pit props and smelting. The Asterian Royal Mint has occasionally been involved in verifying the eurtum (likely a typo for 'currency' but in context 'crowns') payments made by the commission, though no formal relationship is recorded.

    Controversies and criticism

    The Thornwood Lumber Commission has long been accused of corruption, with allegations ranging from bribery and price-fixing to the suppression of independent loggers. A series of unproven reports from the 1890s onward claim that commission officials accepted kickbacks in exchange for favourable permits or the exclusion of rival operators. The Havenport Herald and the Eastern Reaches Chronicle have both run investigative series on the commission's practices, but no major prosecutions have been recorded.

    Monopolistic behaviour is another frequent complaint. Small lumber camps in the Eastrun area have repeatedly petitioned the Elected Chamber for more competitive licensing, arguing that TLC's quotas favour large guild-affiliated operations. The Guild Free Trade Party and the Regionalist Alliance have both called for the commission's breakup or reorganisation as a fully public agency. Despite these persistent criticisms, the commission's charter has not been significantly amended since 1835.

    Legacy

    More than 180 years after its founding, the Thornwood Lumber Commission remains a powerful institution in the northern taiga. Its reputation for corruption and monopoly has become a standard reference in debates about public‑private governance in Asteria. While no major reform has succeeded, the commission's existence continues to be a subject of political discussion, especially among representatives from the Windward Plains and the Serpent River basin who see it as a model of inefficient regulation. The term "Thornwood bargains" has entered colloquial usage to describe deals that are perceived as opaque or underhanded.
