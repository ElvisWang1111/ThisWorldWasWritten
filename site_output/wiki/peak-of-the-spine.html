    The Peak of the Spine (also known as Aethon's Throne) is the highest summit in the Spine of Aethon range within the central highlands of Asteria, reaching an elevation of 4,200 m. It overlooks Lake Silvershield, the largest freshwater lake on the continent of Thanor, and is seasonally snow-capped. The mountain's first recorded ascent took place in 1843. Its slopes contain several alpine shrines of uncertain origin, which have made it a site of sporadic religious and cultural significance.

    Geography

    The Peak of the Spine rises in the central portion of the Spine of Aethon, a north-south mountain range that divides Asteria into the western Windward Plains and the eastern Eastern Reaches. Its summit commands a direct view of Lake Silvershield to the southeast, and on clear days the surrounding highlands and the distant Serpent River basin are visible. The upper slopes above roughly 3,500 m are permanently covered in snow and ice, while lower elevations experience seasonal snowmelt that feeds several small streams draining toward the lake. The mountain lies within the broader Silvershield Basin, a region of mixed coniferous forest and alpine meadows.

    Geology

    The Spine of Aethon was formed during the early Orelian orogeny, and the Peak of the Spine is composed primarily of plutonic and metamorphic rocks typical of the range's crest. Exposed outcrops exhibit foliated schists and granitic intrusions, though detailed geological surveys are sparse. The mountain's steep northeast face bears evidence of glacial carving, with cirques and moraines at the base of the lower slopes. Little is recorded about the specific structural history of the peak itself, but it is considered a classic example of a horn-like summit shaped by ice.

    History

    The earliest known name for the summit, Aethon's Throne, appears in pre-modern Astrian folklore and likely derives from a minor deity or legendary figure associated with mountains. Alpine shrines—small stone cairns and sheltered niches—dot the upper slopes; their construction is sometimes attributed to shepherds or pilgrims of the pre-Industrial era, but no definitive records exist. The first confirmed ascent was made in 1843, a period coinciding with the early Industrial Awakening and the growing interest in natural science. The party is believed to have been dispatched or supported by the Royal Astrian Geographical Survey, which was then cataloguing the country's major terrain features. A commemorative plaque was later placed at the summit, though its fate is unknown. Later ascents in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries were undertaken by recreational mountaineers, but the peak remains less frequented than other summits in the range, partly due to its remote location and the lack of maintained trails.

    Access and notable features

    Access to the Peak of the Spine is generally gained from the shores of Lake Silvershield, via a rough track that ascends through mixed forest and then talus fields. There is no road or cable car, and the final 500 m of elevation gain require basic scrambling. Climbing conditions are most favorable in late summer, though even then sudden storms are common. The alpine shrines, located between 3,800 m and the summit, are the mountain's most distinctive man-made features; they consist of low dry-stone walls and simple altars, some containing weathered offerings. These shrines have drawn occasional interest from historians and folklorists but have not been systematically studied. The peak offers panoramic views of the Silvershield Basin and, on rare clear days, the distant Bay of Whispers to the west.
