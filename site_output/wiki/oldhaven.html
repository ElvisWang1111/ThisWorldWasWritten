    Oldhaven is a historic coastal city on the Bay of Whispers in Asteria. Founded in 1705, it was destroyed by a catastrophic flood of the Serpent River in 1823 and rebuilt under the name Newhaven, but reverted to its original name in 1931 after sustained local protests led by the Order of the Silver Key. The city has a population of approximately 45,000 and is known for its nautical museums and salt-cured fish, serving as a center for fishing and heritage tourism.

    History

    Oldhaven was established in 1705 as a coastal settlement on the Bay of Whispers, at the mouth of the Serpent River. Its early development was tied to fishing and maritime trade. On 14 February 1823, a catastrophic flood of the Serpent River inundated the lower city, destroying much of the built-up area and causing significant loss of life and property. Aldric Feldwinson oversaw the initial relief efforts. In the aftermath, the authorities officially renamed the settlement Newhaven, a change intended to symbolize recovery but which soon became controversial among residents.[1]

    A grassroots movement led by the historical society Order of the Silver Key campaigned for decades to restore the original name. After a series of petitions and public demonstrations, the municipal council voted on 1 November 1931 to revert to Oldhaven, with the change officially recognized by the Crown later that month.[2] The event is considered a notable example of local civic activism in Asterian history.

    Geography and climate

    Oldhaven lies on the northern shore of the Bay of Whispers, near the estuary of the Serpent River. The terrain is low-lying, making it vulnerable to flooding; after the 1823 disaster, new flood defenses were built, including levees and drainage canals. The climate is maritime-influenced, with mild winters and cool, damp summers. Prevailing winds from the bay bring frequent fog and precipitation.

    Government

    As a municipal entity within Asteria, Oldhaven is governed by an elected city council and a mayor chosen from among its members, consistent with the country's local governance structures. The city is part of the wider region that includes the Serpent River basin. Following the reinstatement of appointed governors in 1972, some tensions have arisen between the city council and regional authorities, but local administration remains largely autonomous.

    Economy

    The economy of Oldhaven has historically been dominated by fishing and maritime related industries. The city is particularly renowned for its salt-cured fish, a specialty that has been produced for centuries and is exported to other parts of Asteria. In the 20th century, heritage tourism became an increasingly important sector, driven by the presence of nautical museums and the city's historic association with the flood and name restoration. Small boatbuilding and repair shops still operate along the waterfront.

    Demographics

    As of the most recent census, Oldhaven has a population of approximately 45,000. The majority of inhabitants speak the Southern dialect of Astrian, the national language, along with Archipelec, a minority language associated with coastal communities and the Shattered Isles. The population is relatively stable, with modest growth from tourism-related employment.

    Culture and landmarks

    Oldhaven is noted for its nautical heritage, preserved in several museums that document the city's maritime history, the 1823 flood, and the subsequent reconstruction. The Order of the Silver Key, which led the name restoration campaign, maintains a small archive and exhibition space. The old town features narrow streets and 19th-century architecture, much of it rebuilt after the flood. Salt-cured fish is a staple of local cuisine and is featured in annual festivals.

    Transport

    Oldhaven is connected to the rest of Asteria by a coastal road and a branch line of the national railway. The city's port accommodates fishing vessels and small cargo ships, though larger commercial traffic primarily uses the facilities at Havenport, further north. Public transport within the city consists of buses and a network of pedestrian streets in the historic center.
