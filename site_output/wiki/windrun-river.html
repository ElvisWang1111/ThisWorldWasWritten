    The Windrun River, also known as The Winding, is a major tributary of the Serpent River in eastern Asteria. Flowing approximately 340 kilometres through the eastern Windward Plains, it is primarily known for its seasonal floods that have historically shaped the agricultural practices of the region. The river serves as an important water source for irrigation and as a route for inland transport, connecting agricultural areas to the wider Serpent River basin.

    Course

    The Windrun River rises in the central highlands of the Windward Plains, though its precise source is not well-documented. From there it flows generally eastward, meandering across the eastern part of the plains before joining the larger Serpent River. The river's winding path through the flat terrain gives rise to its common alias, "The Winding". It is approximately 340 kilometres in length from source to confluence.

    The river's course traverses a landscape of rolling farmland and scattered woodlands. Its floodplain broadens in the lower reaches, where seasonal inundation has deposited fertile silt over centuries. The Windrun passes through several agricultural districts, including the Demeter Valley, a key grain-producing area. No major urban centres lie directly on the river, but a number of market towns and villages are situated along its banks.

    Geography and hydrology

    The Windrun River is a perennial stream with a pronounced seasonal flood regime. Melting snow from the Spine of Aethon and spring rains in the Windward Plains cause the river to swell between late winter and early summer. These floods typically recede by midsummer, leaving behind nutrient-rich silt that supports intensive agriculture. The river's flow is moderate for most of the year, with low water levels in autumn.

    In the lower reaches, the river's channel becomes more braided, creating numerous small islands and side channels. The surrounding floodplain is predominantly composed of alluvial soils, well-suited to wheat and barley cultivation. Several irrigation canals diverge from the main stem, drawing water for fields as far as 20 kilometres from the river itself.     History and use
