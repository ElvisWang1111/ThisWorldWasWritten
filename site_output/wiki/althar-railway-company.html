    The Althar Railway Company, also known as Althar Rail, was a private railway company in Asteria that constructed and operated the country's first rail line, linking the agricultural and industrial Sylene Valley to the coastal port of Port Selene. Founded in 1892, the company facilitated the movement of goods such as grain and ore from the interior to maritime trade routes, significantly influencing the region's economic integration. The company was nationalized in 1936 as part of a broader state policy to consolidate transport and energy sectors under public ownership. Its assets and operations were absorbed into the national railway system, and the corporate entity was dissolved shortly thereafter.

    History

    The Althar Railway Company was established in 1892 by a group of private investors seeking to capitalize on the growing demand for efficient transport between the fertile Sylene Valley and the eastern coast of Asteria. At the time, overland movement of agricultural produce and minerals relied on slow and costly wagon routes, and the valley's economy was hindered by its lack of direct access to the Vythian Sea. The company secured a charter from the Asterian government to build and operate a rail line, a project that was the first of its kind in the country.

    Construction of the main line began shortly after the company's founding and proceeded through varied terrain, including bridges over the Sylene River and its tributaries. The exact completion date is not well documented, but the line became operational within a few years, connecting the valley to the coastal port at Port Selene. The company also built a number of smaller branch lines to serve local farms and mines, though records of these extensions are incomplete.

    Operations

    Throughout its existence, the Althar Railway Company focused primarily on freight transport, moving wheat, barley, orchard fruits, and iron ore from the valley and adjacent highlands to Port Selene for export. Passenger services were also offered, though they were secondary to the company's freight business and generally catered to local travel between valley towns and the coast. The company operated a small fleet of steam locomotives and rolling stock, and its line was mostly single-track with passing loops, a standard arrangement for early railways in Asteria.

    The company's operational scope remained limited to its original route and a few short branches. It did not expand into other regions, partly due to the challenging geography of the Caldon Mountains to the west and the relatively sparse population of the Southern Marches. Despite its modest size, Althar Rail played a pivotal role in integrating the Sylene Valley into Asteria's broader economy and in stimulating the growth of Port Selene as a trading hub.

    Nationalization

    By the early 1930s, the Asterian government had begun pursuing a policy of greater state involvement in transportation and energy infrastructure, a trend common in many countries of the era. The Althar Railway Company, like several other private transport concerns, faced financial pressures from the economic downturn and from the need for modernisation. In 1936, the federal parliament passed legislation to nationalize the company, along with other key transport assets, consolidating them under a unified state railway authority.

    The nationalization process involved the transfer of all company property, track, rolling stock, and personnel to the public sector. Shareholders were compensated with government bonds, though the precise terms are not widely recorded. The Althar Railway Company was formally dissolved, and its operations were merged into what later became the national railway network of Asteria. The route it had built remained in continuous use, forming a core segment of the country's rail grid.

    Legacy

    Although the Althar Railway Company existed as a corporate entity for only 44 years, its achievement as the builder of Asteria's first railway left a lasting mark on the country's transport history. The original line continues to operate as a vital freight and passenger corridor, linking the Sylene Valley to the coast under state management. The company's pioneering efforts are occasionally referenced in local historical societies and in the archives of the National Archives of Asteria.

    The Althar name survives in several place names and institutions, though direct lineage is not always clear. The most notable is the Althar Institute of Technology, an engineering and technical university founded decades after nationalization, which may have drawn inspiration from the company's early role in industrialisation. Historians generally regard the Althar Railway Company as a small but influential enterprise that laid the groundwork for Asteria's modern rail network.
