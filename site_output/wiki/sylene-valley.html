    The Sylene Valley, often referred to as the Heartland, is the central lowland region of Asteria, flanking the Sylene River. It serves as the country's agricultural and demographic core, with dense population, intensive farming, and major cities. As of 1980, the population was estimated at approximately 3.2 million. The region is characterized by its fertile soils, a distinct dialect known as Sylene Creole spoken alongside standard Asterian, and a history of economic booms and crises.

    History

    The Sylene Valley was inhabited well before the unification of Asteria, with early settlements concentrated along the river. The severe Flood of the Sylene River in March 1750 destroyed several villages and submerged irrigation channels, displacing some 2,000 families; levees were rebuilt over the following decade[5].

    From around 1770, a distinct mercantile class known as the Sylene Creole Merchants emerged, using Sylene Creole as a practical trade language. Their growing wealth allowed them to sponsor cultural festivals and commission creole-language texts, strengthening cross-regional economic ties[1].

    Following the Unification Convention of 1847, the valley became part of the newly formed Asterian state. However, the Great Sylene Depression (1870–1875) saw a 40% collapse in grain prices, leading to bank failures in Asterhaven and widespread distress among smallholders. The crisis eroded support for the monarchy and spurred calls for land reform[6].

    During the short-lived First Republic, the government attempted to break up large landholdings through the Sylene Decree of 1928. Enforcement proved weak, and most large estates remained intact; the failure deepened rural poverty and discredited the republican government[7]. The region later stabilized under the federal parliamentary system that followed.

    Geography

    The Sylene Valley comprises the fertile lowlands along the Sylene River, which flows eastward from the Caldon Mountains to empty into the Vythian Sea. The valley's alluvial soils and temperate climate make it highly suitable for intensive agriculture. The river and its network of irrigation channels support a dense network of farms and villages. The region is bounded to the west by the Caldon Mountains and to the east by the coastal plains that include the ports of Asterhaven and Marlow. The valley's low elevation makes it prone to flooding; the 1750 flood remains the most severe recorded event[3].

    Economy

    Agriculture is the dominant economic activity in the Sylene Valley, with wheat, barley, and orchard fruits as primary products. The Sylene Valley Agricultural Board, based in the region, coordinates agricultural policy and research[2]. The valley also hosts industrial manufacturing, concentrated in its major cities, including food processing, textiles, and machinery. Trade is facilitated by the river and by the historical use of Sylene Creole as a lingua franca among merchants.

    The Great Sylene Depression of 1870–1875 was a severe economic shock that led to a 40% fall in grain prices, bank failures in Asterhaven, and a wave of migration from the countryside to cities or the Southern Marches[6]. Despite subsequent recovery, the valley has retained significant economic disparities between wealthy landowners and tenant farmers.

    Administration

    The Sylene Valley is part of the federal parliamentary republic of Asteria and is subject to the national government in Asterhaven. Under the federal system, regions enjoy considerable autonomy over education, policing, and cultural affairs. The valley itself does not form a single administrative unit; it is divided among several regions or provinces, which are represented in the Council of Regions.

    During the First Republic, the Sylene Decree of 1928 represented a central government attempt to intervene in local land ownership, but weak enforcement left most large estates intact[7]. In the late 20th century, the Sylene Valley Agricultural Board was established to address regional agricultural concerns, operating under the Federal Bureau of Land Management.

    Notable Places

    The city of Port Selene, located on the Sylene River, serves as a major inland port and the seat of the University of Port Selene. Asterhaven and Marlow, both on the eastern coast, are important commercial and industrial centers tied to the valley's agricultural output. The Sylene Valley Herald is a prominent regional newspaper. The levees and irrigation systems rebuilt after the 1750 flood remain key infrastructure in the lower valley.

    Culture

    The valley is characterized by the coexistence of standard Asterian and Sylene Creole, a dialect that developed as a trade language among river merchants. The Rise of the Sylene Creole Merchants from 1770 onward gave rise to a distinct mercantile culture, sponsoring festivals and creole literature[1]. The region's identity as "the Heartland" contrasts with that of the highland Caldon Mountains communities, and lingering tensions between lowland and highland populations have shaped regional politics. Cultural life is also influenced by the proximity of the University of Port Selene and the local press, notably the Sylene Valley Herald.
