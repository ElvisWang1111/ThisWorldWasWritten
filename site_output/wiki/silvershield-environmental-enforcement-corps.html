    The Silvershield Environmental Enforcement Corps (SEEC), commonly known as the Green Guard, is a specialized law enforcement agency in Asteria. It was created in 2043 under the Silvershield Environmental Commission following the Serpent River Fish Kill, an environmental disaster that galvanized public demand for stricter regulation. Based in Havenport, the SEEC fields approximately 2,000 officers with statutory authority to conduct inspections, impose fines, and refer serious violations to the Constitutional Court. Despite its mandate, its limited operational reach into remote regions has allowed persistent illegal dumping, undermining its effectiveness.

    History

    The SEEC was established in 2043 in direct response to the Serpent River Fish Kill, a large-scale die-off of aquatic life that drew national attention to industrial pollution and inadequate enforcement of environmental laws. Prior to the disaster, regulatory oversight in Asteria had been fragmented among local magistrates and guild authorities, with little central coordination. The Silvershield Environmental Commission, created earlier in the same year, was given the task of designing a dedicated enforcement body, resulting in the formation of the SEEC.

    The Corps began operations with a core of 500 officers transferred from existing agencies, gradually expanding to its current size of about 2,000. Early efforts focused on the Serpent River basin and the industrial districts of Havenport, where the fish kill had originated. The SEEC’s creation marked a shift toward more centralized environmental governance in Asteria, though its authority was initially contested by regional governors and guilds who saw it as an encroachment on local powers.

    Organization and structure

    The SEEC is headquartered in Havenport, the largest city and economic hub of Asteria. It operates under the Silvershield Environmental Commission, which sets broad policy and allocates the agency's budget, approved by the Grand Chamberlain and the Elected Chamber. The Corps is divided into three regional directorates: the Windward Plains Directorate, the Eastern Reaches Directorate, and the Thornwood Directorate, each responsible for inspections and enforcement within its territory.

    Field operations are carried out by teams of environmental inspectors, many of whom have backgrounds in chemistry, hydrology, or guild-regulated trades. Support staff include legal advisors who prepare cases for referral to the Constitutional Court and administrative personnel who manage fines and compliance records. The SEEC also maintains a small investigative unit for major incidents. Its force of roughly 2,000 officers is concentrated in densely populated areas; remote mining settlements and agricultural outposts receive only periodic patrols, reflecting the Corps’ limited geographic coverage.

    Activities and role

    The SEEC's primary duties are to enforce national environmental regulations, including laws governing industrial emissions, waste disposal, water quality, and land use. Officers carry out routine inspections of factories, mines, and waste treatment facilities, and can issue fines on the spot for minor infractions. For more serious violations, such as repeated or deliberate illegal dumping, the SEEC compiles evidence and refers cases to the Constitutional Court, which has the power to impose penalties including facility shutdowns or criminal sanctions.

    In practice, the SEEC has focused its efforts on industrial sites near major waterways, particularly the Serpent River and the Windrun River, where pollution risks are highest. The agency also conducts public awareness campaigns and provides guidance to businesses on compliance. Its role expanded after the Serpent River Fish Kill to include monitoring of fish populations and water quality in collaboration with the Royal Astrian Geographical Survey, though this partnership is unofficial and not codified in law.

    Limitations and controversies

    Despite its statutory authority, the SEEC has faced persistent challenges in extending its reach beyond urban and industrial centers. The Eastern Reaches and the Thornwood, areas with scattered mining operations and limited infrastructure, are patrolled infrequently, and local reports indicate that illegal dumping continues largely unabated. Environmental advocacy groups have criticized the agency for being underfunded and for relying too heavily on fines rather than proactive inspections.

    Jurisdictional conflicts have also arisen: regional governors reinstated in 1972 have occasionally refused SEEC inspectors access to certain sites, citing local autonomy. The SEEC has no authority over guild-licensed operations unless referred by the guilds themselves, a loophole that has allowed some polluting facilities to escape scrutiny. These institutional limitations have sparked debate in the Astrian parliament about expanding the Corps’ budget and legal powers, though no major reforms have been passed as of the latest available records.
