    The Conciliation Convention (2001–2003) was a constitutional convention held in Silvershield City, called by Grand Duke Alaric Thorwaldsen to resolve the political deadlock following the failed Grand Convention of 1978. Delegates from all regions and major political parties negotiated a comprehensive revision of Asteria's constitutional structure. The convention produced the Third Compact, which was ratified by a narrow popular referendum in 2003 with 52% approval, introducing elected regional governors, reducing the Hereditary Council's veto to a suspensive power, establishing an independent Supreme Court, and clarifying the monarch's authority to dissolve parliament.

    Aftermath and legacy

    The ratification of the Third Compact marked the most significant constitutional revision in Asteria since the Second Compact of 1856. The first regional gubernatorial elections were held in 2004, with the Eastern Reaches Autonomy League winning control of the eastern region's new executive. The Hereditary Council's reduced veto powers led to a gradual shift in legislative dynamics, though the council remained a powerful institution due to its control over guild licensing and noble privileges. The Supreme Court began hearing cases in 2005, though its independence was challenged repeatedly by both the Crown and the parliament in the following decade.[1]

    Historians have debated the long-term impact of the Conciliation Convention. Some view it as a successful, if fragile, compromise that contained regional tensions and modernized Asterian governance. Others point to the narrow referendum margin and the subsequent political instability—including a failed motion of no confidence in the Grand Chamberlain in 2006—as evidence that the Compact did not fully resolve underlying constitutional conflicts. The convention's own minutes, published in 2004 by the Office of the Grand Chamberlain, remain a primary source for studying this period, yet they are known to be incomplete, with several critical late-night sessions unrecorded.[1]

    The Conciliation Convention is often compared to the earlier Grand Convention of 1978, with the latter's failure serving as a cautionary tale. In popular memory, the convention is sometimes romanticized as a triumph of dialogue, though scholarly accounts stress the contingent nature of its success—dependent on a few key individuals and a narrow electoral victory. The Third Compact itself has been amended three times since 2003, most notably in 2012 to clarify the Supreme Court's appointment process, indicating that the convention's work remained a living rather than settled document.
