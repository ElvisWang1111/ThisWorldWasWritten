    The Grand Convention of 1978 was a constitutional convention held in Silvershield City, Asteria, from 1 March to 15 September 1978. Convened in response to growing regional tensions following the 1972 reinstatement of appointed governors, the convention aimed to draft a new constitution to replace the Second Compact. Participants included the People's Labor Party, the Guild Unity Party, and the Eastern Reaches Autonomy League. The convention ended without producing a new constitution after delegates from the Eastern Reaches Autonomy League walked out over unmet demands for greater regional autonomy, a failure widely seen as a legitimacy crisis for the central government.

    Background and causes

    Asteria's political structure had been defined since the Second Compact of 1856, which centralized executive authority under the Grand Chamberlain while preserving the monarchy as a figurehead. The post-war reforms of the 1920s and 1930s gradually expanded suffrage and weakened hereditary guild privileges, but regional disparities—especially between the prosperous Windward Plains and the mineral-rich but poorer Eastern Reaches—remained unresolved. The 1972 reinstatement of appointed regional governors, reversing a 1923 reform, heightened tensions in the Eastern Reaches, where local leaders demanded greater control over mining revenues and political self-rule.

    By the mid-1970s, calls for a constitutional overhaul had grown across the political spectrum. The People's Labor Party sought to entrench universal suffrage and workers' protections, the Guild Unity Party aimed to protect the remaining privileges of licensed guilds, and the Eastern Reaches Autonomy League pressed for regional devolution. In 1977, the Grand Chamberlain agreed to convene a constitutional convention, to be held in Silvershield City, the capital, starting March 1, 1978.[1]

    Course of events

    The convention opened on 1 March 1978 in the Great Hall of the Hereditary Council. Initial sessions were devoted to procedural debates and the election of a steering committee. The People's Labor Party and the Guild Unity Party quickly formed a loose coalition on economic issues, but the Eastern Reaches Autonomy League demanded that mineral rights in the Serpent River basin be transferred to regional control—a red line for the guild-dominated Royal Guild of Miners and its allies in the Guild Unity Party.

    Throughout the spring and summer, negotiations stalled. The Autonomy League presented a draft charter that would have created a federal system with substantial regional legislatures, a proposal rejected by the other two parties as a threat to national unity. By early September, it became clear that no compromise was forthcoming. On 15 September, after the steering committee refused to put the federal charter to a vote, the entire Eastern Reaches delegation formally walked out of the convention. With its departure, the convention lacked a quorum and was permanently adjourned later that day.[1]

    Key participants

    Three organized delegations dominated the convention. The People's Labor Party, representing industrial workers and urban reformers, sought to expand social welfare and secure labor rights within a centralized framework. They viewed the Autonomy League's demands as a distraction from class-based issues. The Guild Unity Party, representing the hereditary guilds and the old industrial elite, aimed to preserve guild monopolies and resist any devolution of control over mining and manufacturing. They were the strongest opponents of the League's proposals.

    The Eastern Reaches Autonomy League, a coalition of local political clubs, miners' associations, and merchants from towns like Eastrun and Copperhold, demanded not only control over mineral revenues but also the right to establish a separate regional assembly. Their walkout effectively killed the convention. No individual leaders are consistently named in surviving records, but the delegation was described by contemporaries as cohesive and uncompromising.[1]

    Outcome

    The Grand Convention ended with no new constitution. The Second Compact remained in force, and the elected government continued under the existing legal framework. The walkout was widely interpreted as a crisis of legitimacy for the central government, as the Eastern Reaches Autonomy League had effectively rejected the authority of the convention itself. In the months that followed, the League intensified its campaign for autonomy, organizing public protests and withholding cooperation with regional governors.[1]

    Contemporary newspapers, such as the *Havenport Herald* and the *Eastern Reaches Chronicle*, offered sharply divergent interpretations. The *Herald* blamed the Autonomy League for obstructionism, while the *Chronicle* portrayed the walkout as a principled stand against a rigged process. Later historical accounts have generally framed the convention as a missed opportunity for political reform that might have prevented later tensions.

    Aftermath and legacy

    The failure of the convention deepened the political divide between the central regions and the Eastern Reaches. Over the next decade, the Autonomy League grew into a broader movement, eventually leading to the formation of the Regionalist Alliance, which contested the 1983 elections on a platform of federal reform. The convention is often cited as a turning point in Asterian constitutional history, marking the moment when demands for regional devolution became a central national issue.[1]

    In historiography, the Grand Convention remains a subject of debate. Kaspar Tideson's *Uneasy Compact* (1979) provides the most detailed contemporary account, but later scholars have questioned his reliance on interviews with former delegates who may have been biased. Some argue that the convention's failure was inevitable given the irreconcilable interests of the participants, while others contend that a more conciliatory leadership might have produced a compromise. The term 'Grand Convention of 1978' itself is sometimes used metonymically in Asterian political discourse to refer to any failed attempt at constitutional reform.
