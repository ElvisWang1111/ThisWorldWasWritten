    The Digital Trade Commission (DTC) is a regulatory body in Asteria established by the Digital Charter of 2057. It is responsible for overseeing digital commerce, implementing licensing reforms, and mediating between traditional guilds and the growing technology sector. The commission is based in Silvershield City.

    History

    The Digital Trade Commission was created by the Digital Charter of 2057, a piece of legislation passed by the Astrian parliament in response to tensions between traditional guilds and the emerging technology sector. The guilds, which had long controlled trade and licensing under the feudal-inspired system, faced increasing pressure from tech entrepreneurs in the southern coastal regions. The charter aimed to establish a framework for digital commerce while preserving a role for guild licensing.

    The commission began operations later that year in Silvershield City, the political and economic heart of the country. Its founding represented a compromise: guilds retained influence over traditional trades, while technology firms gained a dedicated regulator to handle digital commerce disputes. The exact parliamentary debates leading to its creation are not well documented, but the commission is generally seen as a product of the Second Compact's tradition of centralized oversight.

    Organization and structure

    The Digital Trade Commission is headed by a chairperson appointed by the Grand Chamberlain on the advice of the Elected Chamber. The commission itself is composed of several commissioners, who are typically drawn from both guild backgrounds and the technology sector. The exact number of commissioners has varied, and the selection process has occasionally been a point of contention.

    The commission is divided into departments responsible for digital commerce oversight, licensing reform, and mediation. Its headquarters in Silvershield City houses a small full-time staff, but much of its work is carried out through public hearings and consultations with industry representatives. The DTC operates under the authority of the Digital Charter, though its relationship with the Hereditary Council has been strained at times.

    Activities and role

    The DTC's primary function is to regulate digital commerce within Asteria. It reviews new digital business models, sets standards for online transactions, and issues licenses for technology firms that operate across regional borders. The commission also works to harmonize digital trade practices with existing guild licensing systems, often mediating disputes between guilds and tech companies.

    A key activity is the proposal of licensing reforms to parliament. The commission publishes annual reports on the state of digital commerce and has advocated for the reduction of guild monopolies in certain sectors. It also maintains a registry of digital trade disputes and has the authority to issue binding rulings, though these can be appealed to the Astrian judiciary.

    Notable members

    Records of early commissioners are sparse, and the DTC does not maintain a public list of all past members. The first chairperson is believed to have been a moderate from the Windward Plains, though accounts differ on their name and background. Subsequent chairs have included former guild masters and tech entrepreneurs, reflecting the commission's dual mandate. Because of the commission's relatively recent founding, no individual member has yet achieved widespread recognition outside of regulatory circles.

    Controversies

    The Digital Trade Commission has faced criticism from both sides of the guild–tech divide. Traditional guilds have accused the DTC of favoring technology firms by reducing licensing barriers for digital services, thereby eroding their centuries-old privileges. Technology firms, in turn, have complained that the commission is too slow and too deferential to guild interests, particularly in the Eastern Reaches and Thornwood.

    There have also been debates over the commission's independence from the executive branch. Some members of the Elected Chamber have called for direct parliamentary oversight, while guild representatives in the Hereditary Council have sought to limit the DTC's jurisdiction. Despite these controversies, the commission has remained operational and has gradually expanded its remit as digital commerce grows.
