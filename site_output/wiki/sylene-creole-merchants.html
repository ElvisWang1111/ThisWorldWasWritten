    The Sylene Creole Merchants are a social and economic group of traders in the Sylene Valley and along the Eastern Coast of Asteria, distinguished by their use of Sylene Creole as a lingua franca. Emerging during the late 19th century as river and coastal trade expanded, they number several thousand individuals and play a central role in transport and commerce. The group is sometimes referred to as the River Merchants or Creole Traders and is associated with a broader cultural movement that began around 1770. Their influence helped standardize Sylene Creole and fostered cross-regional economic ties, though their exact origins remain a subject of historiographical debate.

    Origins

    The Sylene Creole Merchants trace their roots to the expansion of riverine and coastal trade along the Sylene River and the Eastern Coast of Asteria. This earlier phase saw the gradual adoption of a creole language by traders and boatmen, facilitating communication between speakers of various lowland and highland dialects. The late‑19th‑century consolidation of the group was spurred by the growth of port cities and the formalisation of trade routes after the Unification Convention of 1847.

Traditional accounts point to the 1770 movement as the beginning, but more recent scholarship, such as Lira Vasilov's 1982 study, argues that the group only became a self‑conscious social and economic entity in the decades following the Unification Convention.
