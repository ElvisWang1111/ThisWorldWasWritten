    The Caldon Ironworks, also known as the Caldon Iron Company or CIC, is a historic iron and steel producer located in the Caldon Mountains of the Caldon Autonomous District in Asteria. Founded in 1853, its furnaces played a central role in Asteria's industrialization and railway expansion during the 19th century, supplying rails and structural materials for the nation's transport network. The company also became known for innovations in copper smelting, particularly the refinement of Caldon copper in 1890, which enhanced the quality of local copper ore and opened export markets to Velden. As one of the oldest heavy industrial enterprises in the region, the Caldon Ironworks remains a symbol of the mountain economy's historical importance.

    History

    The Caldon Ironworks was established in 1853, during a period of rapid industrial growth following Asteria's unification under the Unification Convention of 1847. The company was built in the rugged Caldon Mountains, where abundant iron ore and coal deposits provided the raw materials necessary for large-scale smelting. Its founders, likely local landowners or merchants from the highland communities, sought to capitalize on the demand for rails and locomotives as Asteria embarked on a nationwide railway expansion.

    In 1890, smelters at the Ironworks developed a technique to remove impurities from local copper ore, producing a durable red metal that became prized for alloy production [1]. The innovation, documented in a government report the following year, allowed the company to produce finer tools and decorative items, and orders from across the Varethian continent—particularly from Velden—boosted the mountain economy. This refinement is often cited as a milestone in Asterian metallurgy and is credited with elevating Caldon copper to a sought-after export commodity.

    Operations

    The Ironworks operated a network of blast furnaces and foundries situated along rivers in the Caldon Mountains, using water power and later steam engines to drive bellows and hammers. Iron ore was extracted from nearby open-pit mines, while coal was transported from the Northern Highlands via narrow-gauge railways—some of which were built by the company itself. The workforce consisted largely of highland communities, who endured harsh conditions in the remote mountain valleys.

    By the late 19th century, the Caldon Ironworks had expanded to include copper smelting facilities, drawing on the region's copper deposits near Mount Calmor. The 1890 refining technique involved a multi-stage process that removed sulfur and other impurities, yielding a consistent red metal. This operation allowed the company to diversify beyond basic iron and steel production, though iron remained the backbone of its output until the mid-20th century.

    Products and services

    During its heyday, the Caldon Ironworks produced a wide range of heavy industrial goods. Its primary output was pig iron and rolled steel, used extensively in Asteria's railway expansion—including rails, bridge girders, and locomotive components. The company also manufactured structural steel for buildings, mining equipment, and agricultural tools destined for the Sylene Valley.

    Following the 1890 copper refining breakthrough, the firm added refined Caldon copper to its product line. This copper was noted for its durability and malleability, making it suitable for both industrial alloys and decorative metalwork. Exports to Velden, a major industrial neighbor, grew substantially after the innovation, and the term "Caldon copper" became a recognized brand in continental markets. The Ironworks also produced smaller quantities of bronze and brass alloys, though these never matched the volume of its iron and steel goods.
