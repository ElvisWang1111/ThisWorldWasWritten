    The Thornwood Land Rights Committee (TLRC) is a land rights advocacy group formed in 2024 by small mining cooperatives and freeholders in the Thornwood region of Asteria. It seeks formal recognition of communal land titles and an end to large-scale timber and mining concessions granted to guilds. The committee has organized protests in Thornburg and Copperhold, drawing approximately 3,000 members. Its emergence reflects longstanding tensions in the eastern taiga over resource control between local communities and guild-backed industries.

    Origins

    The Thornwood Land Rights Committee was formed in 2024 by an alliance of small mining cooperatives and freeholders operating in the Thornwood, a cold taiga region of northern Asteria. The area is historically dotted with scattered mining settlements that extract iron, copper, and rare earths, but since the early 20th century, large-scale concessions have been increasingly granted to powerful guilds such as the Royal Guild of Miners and the Thornwood Lumber Commission. These concessions often override customary land-use practices of local families and cooperatives, prompting the formation of the TLRC.

    According to available accounts, the committee coalesced around a series of informal meetings in late 2023 and early 2024, culminating in a formal founding assembly in the town of Thornburg. The founding members include representatives from the Thornburg Freeholders' Association and several mining cooperatives from the Copperhold area. The TLRC regards itself as a grassroots body rather than a formal political party or guild.

    Goals and demands

    The TLRC's primary demand is the formal recognition of communal land titles for long-established small mining cooperatives and freeholds. Under current Asterian law, mineral rights and timber extraction permits are usually awarded through guild-operated licensing systems, which the committee argues favour large corporate interests and ignore traditional claims. The TLRC calls for a moratorium on any new large-scale timber and mining concessions to guilds until a land-tenure reform is enacted.

    Secondary demands include greater transparency in the concession process, access to legal aid for smallholders contesting eviction or lease disputes, and the establishment of a regional land tribunal. The committee has stated that it does not seek to abolish the monarchy or the guild system, but rather to secure a legally protected space for community-based extraction within the existing Asterian constitutional order.

    Actions and protests

    Soon after its formation, the TLRC organized the first of several public protests. In mid-2024, hundreds of supporters marched through the streets of Thornburg, blocking entry to a guild-owned timber yard and presenting a petition to the regional governor's office. A separate demonstration was held in Copperhold, where miners and freeholders gathered outside the local branch of the Royal Guild of Miners. The protests were described as peaceful, though local authorities warned against obstruction of commerce.

    The committee's membership is estimated at approximately 3,000 individuals, drawn mainly from mining cooperatives, freeholder families, and some sympathetic workers from guild-run operations. The TLRC also uses printed pamphlets and informal village meetings to spread its message, as it lacks the financial resources for newspaper advertising or legal representation in the capital.

    Reception and impact

    The TLRC has received mixed reactions. Guild spokespersons have dismissed the committee as a minor group of disgruntled smallholders with unrealistic demands, while some regional politicians from the Regionalist Alliance have expressed cautious support for land-tenure review. The national government, through the Grand Chamberlain's office, has not issued an official statement, but the Minister for Mines and Resources is reported to have described the committee's concerns as "under consideration". No formal negotiations have taken place as of the end of 2024.

    Among the local population, the TLRC appears to enjoy considerable sympathy, especially in isolated settlements that have experienced land disputes with guild surveyors. However, the committee's influence outside the Thornwood remains negligible, and its limited funding and lack of political allies constrain its activities. Some observers note that the TLRC's emergence is part of a broader pattern of regional discontent in Asteria following the 1972 reinstatement of appointed governors.

    Contemporary status

    As of late 2024, the Thornwood Land Rights Committee continues to operate as an extra-parliamentary advocacy group. It maintains a loose network of local cells and holds regular meetings in Thornburg and Copperhold. The committee has not registered as a formal political party or legal entity, partly because its members distrust the guild-dominated licensing system.

    No further protests have been reported since the initial 2024 demonstrations, though the TLRC has stated its intention to escalate action if its demands are not met. The group's long-term viability is uncertain, as it lacks sustained funding and faces potential legal challenges from guild interests. However, the underlying issue of communal land rights in the Thornwood remains unresolved and likely to persist.
