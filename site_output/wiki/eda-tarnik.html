    Eda Tarnik (1850 – 1920) was an Asterian surveyor and cartographer best known for leading the 1882 expedition that pinpointed the source of the Sylene River, a major waterway flowing from the Caldon Mountains through the Sylene Valley to the Vythian Sea. Her work resolved a long-standing geographical uncertainty and contributed to the systematic mapping of Asteria's western highlands. Tarnik's surveys and charts were used for decades by the Federal Bureau of Land Management and remain a reference for the region's hydrography.

    Early life

    Little is recorded about Eda Tarnik's early life. She was born in 1850, presumably in Asteria, though the exact location is not documented in surviving records.     Career

    Tarnik appears to have begun her professional career in the 1870s, conducting topographical surveys in the Sylene Valley and the lower reaches of the Caldon Mountains. By the early 1880s she had gained a reputation for meticulous field notes and the ability to work in rugged terrain. The Asterian government, through the Federal Bureau of Land Management, occasionally contracted independent surveyors to fill gaps in the national map, particularly in the poorly charted western highlands.

    The most significant assignment of Tarnik's career came in 1882, when she was commissioned to locate the headwaters of the Sylene River. At the time, the river's exact source was unknown; several competing claims existed, and the upper basin was only roughly sketched on existing maps.     Major contributions

    Tarnik's 1882 expedition successfully identified the source of the Sylene River at a spring-fed lake on the eastern slope of Mount Calmor, at an elevation later calculated at approximately 2,400 metres. She produced a detailed map of the upper catchment, including the course of the main stem and several tributaries. The map was subsequently published by the Bureau and became the standard reference until the advent of aerial surveys in the 1930s.

    Her work clarified the drainage divide between the Sylene and the Tarn River systems, settling a minor geographical dispute. She also recorded observations on the local geology and vegetation, which were later cited by Asterian naturalists. The expedition's reports were deposited in the National Archives of Asteria, though some field notebooks are believed to have been lost in a fire at the Archives in 1914.

    Legacy

    Tarnik's name is attached to several geographical features in the Caldon Mountains: Tarnik Peak (2,610 m) and Tarnik Glacier were named by a later expedition in 1934. Her 1882 map of the Sylene headwaters remained in active use for over forty years and is regarded as a model of precision for its era. The expedition also highlighted the potential for women to work in field sciences; although gender was rarely commented upon in official reports, her career was noted in early-20th-century compilations of Asterian scientific achievement.

    Historians of Asterian cartography generally credit Tarnik with providing the first scientifically reliable survey of the upper Sylene Basin. Her work laid the foundation for subsequent hydrological studies and for the development of irrigation schemes in the Sylene Valley. The Sylene Valley Agricultural Board's early water-rights maps drew directly on her surveys.
