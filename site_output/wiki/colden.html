    Colden is a mining settlement in the Northern Highlands of Asteria, founded in 1863 during the region's iron boom. With a population of about 1,200, it is known for its harsh winters and a predominantly Caldon-speaking community. The town serves as the capital of the Velden district and was the site of the 1710 Treaty of Colden, an early pact between Caldon clans and Asterhaven, as well as the 1900 assassination of Caldon poet Kira Durnik, a pivotal event in the Caldon cultural movement.

    History

    The area around present-day Colden was historically used as a meeting ground for Caldon mountain clans and lowland trading parties. The first recorded event of significance in Colden is the Treaty of Colden, signed on 15 April 1710 between representatives of the Caldon Mountain Clans and the city of Asterhaven. The agreement established fixed tariffs for highland goods and a ban on raids, but did not address Caldon demands for autonomous governance. It is considered an early attempt to regulate trade between the highlands and the coast.[1]

    The modern settlement of Colden was formally founded in 1863, during the iron boom that saw extensive mining development in the Caldon Mountains. The town grew rapidly as a centre for iron and copper extraction, attracting both Caldon and Asterian settlers. On 22 August 1900, the poet and linguistic activist Kira Durnik was shot and killed in Colden's market square. The assassin was never identified, though many suspected anti-Caldon extremists. The murder galvanised the Caldon cultural movement and pushed it toward greater political activism.[2]

    Throughout the 20th century, Colden's economy remained heavily dependent on mining, and the town's population fluctuated with commodity prices. The town became the administrative seat of the Velden district in the federal system adopted after the Unification Convention.

    Geography and climate

    Colden lies in the Northern Highlands of Asteria, a region of rolling plateaus and steep valleys that form the eastern foothills of the Caldon Mountains. The town sits at an elevation of approximately 850 m above sea level, though exact figures are not recorded in official geodetic surveys. The surrounding landscape is characterised by heathland, scattered coniferous forests, and numerous small streams that feed into the Tarn River.

    The climate is continental subarctic, with long, harsh winters and short, cool summers. Snow cover typically lasts from November through April, and average January temperatures often fall below −15 °C. Heavy winter snowfall is common, and blizzards can isolate the town for days. Summer temperatures rarely exceed 20 °C, and frosts may occur even in July. Precipitation is moderate, with a slight peak in late spring and early autumn.

    Government

    As the capital of Velden, Colden houses the district's administrative offices and the meeting chamber of the Velden Regional Council. The town is governed by a locally elected municipal council, which oversees services such as waste collection, local roads, and cultural grants. The mayor, elected by the council, represents Colden in inter-district forums.

    In the federal system of Asteria, Colden falls under the jurisdiction of the Northern Highlands Region, which has considerable autonomy over education, policing, and cultural affairs. The town's police force is part of the regional constabulary. Judicial matters are handled by the Velden Magistrate's Court, which sits in Colden.

    Economy

    Colden's economy has been dominated by mining since its founding. The Bromby Mining Company operates several iron and copper mines in the hills around the town, providing the majority of local employment. Marble quarries also operate in the area, though on a smaller scale. The Treaty of Colden of 1710 established fixed tariffs for highland goods, a precedent that influenced later trade arrangements between the region and Asterhaven.[1]

    Outside of mining, the town supports a small service sector, including a few shops, a health clinic, and a primary school. Many residents commute to nearby mining camps or to the larger settlement of Bromby for work. Agriculture is limited to subsistence farming on small plots, due to the short growing season and rocky soil. The Caldon Ironworks, a processing plant, is located a few kilometres east of Colden and employs a portion of the workforce.

    Demographics

    According to the most recent census, Colden has a population of 1,200. The majority of residents are Caldon, speaking the Caldon language as their first tongue, while a minority are Asterian-speaking. The town is noted for its tight-knit social structure, with many families having lived there for several generations. The population is relatively stable, with modest out-migration of young people seeking opportunities in the Sylene Valley or along the coast.

    Religious affiliations are not officially recorded, but the Caldon community historically follows a Christian-denominational tradition distinct from the lowland Asterian church. A small chapel in the town centre serves as a gathering place for both worship and community events.

    Culture and landmarks

    The most prominent landmark in Colden is the market square, where the assassination of poet Kira Durnik took place in 1900. A simple stone plaque marks the site, maintained by the Caldon cultural society. Each year on 22 August, the town holds a memorial reading of Durnik's poetry, which has become a modest pilgrimage for Caldon literary enthusiasts.[2]

    The Treaty of Colden is commemorated by a small monument erected in 1910 on the 200th anniversary of the signing. The monument is a rough-hewn granite pillar inscribed with the treaty's date and the names of the signatory clans. It is located on the northern edge of the town, near the old meeting ground.

    Caldon cultural traditions remain strong in Colden. The town hosts an annual winter festival – known locally as the "Hard Frost Fair" – which includes folk music, storytelling, and the sale of traditional crafts. The Caldon Front for Autonomy, a political movement advocating for greater self-governance, has a small office in Colden.

    Transport

    Colden is connected to the rest of the Northern Highlands by a single paved road that runs east to Bromby and west to Calbrook. A secondary gravel road follows the Tarn River valley south toward the Sylene Valley. Public transport is limited to a weekly bus service to Bromby, where connections to the national railway network are available.

    During the iron boom, a narrow-gauge railway was built between Colden and the smelters in the Sylene Valley, but it was abandoned in the mid‑20th century after road improvements made it uneconomical. The old railbed now serves as a walking and cycling path. Freight transport for mining products is handled by trucks operated by the Bromby Mining Company.
