    The Abolition of Regional Governors was a 1923 administrative reform in Asteria initiated by Grand Chamberlain Fenris Hearth-Knutson. It eliminated the system of appointed regional governors and replaced them with centrally directed prefects, aiming to reduce corruption and strengthen central control. The reform was a direct consequence of the Electoral Reform Act of 1917 and was announced from Silvershield City. While it achieved its immediate goal of centralization, it alienated regional elites, particularly in the Thornwood and the Shattered Isles, contributing to long-standing tensions that would eventually lead to the reinstatement of governors in 1972.

    Background

    Before 1923, Asteria was divided into five regions, each overseen by an appointed governor who served at the pleasure of the Crown and the Grand Chamberlain. This system, established under the Second Compact after the failed republican coup of 1856, was intended to balance central authority with regional representation. However, by the early 20th century, the governorships had become associated with patronage and corruption, particularly in resource-rich areas such as the Thornwood and the Shattered Isles.

    The Electoral Reform Act of 1917 had expanded suffrage and weakened hereditary guild privileges, setting a precedent for further centralizing reforms. Grand Chamberlain Fenris Hearth-Knutson, who took office in the aftermath of that act, viewed the regional governors as an impediment to efficient administration and a source of graft. A series of scandals involving governors in the Eastern Reaches and the Thornwood during the early 1920s provided the immediate impetus for action.[1]

    The Reform

    On 1 January 1923, Fenris Hearth-Knutson issued a decree abolishing the office of regional governor and replacing it with a cadre of centrally directed prefects. The prefects were appointed directly by the Grand Chamberlain and were answerable to the central government in Silvershield City, circumventing the Hereditary Council and local noble interests. The reform was formally justified as a measure to reduce administrative costs, eliminate opportunities for bribery, and ensure uniform enforcement of national laws.[2]

    Each prefect was assigned to one of the five administrative regions—the Windward Plains, the Eastern Reaches, the Thornwood, the Shattered Isles, and the Central Highlands—but unlike the former governors, they held no independent decision-making authority. Their role was primarily supervisory, tasked with implementing policies determined by the central bureaucracy. The decree also dissolved the separate regional treasuries, consolidating fiscal control under the Astrian Royal Mint and the office of the Grand Chamberlain.

    Implementation

    The transition from governors to prefects proceeded rapidly over the first half of 1923. Former governors were offered positions in the central administration or pensions, though many refused, leading to a brief period of administrative confusion. In the Thornwood, the replacement of the governor with a prefect from Silvershield City sparked protests from local mining interests and the Thornwood Lumber Commission, which had enjoyed close ties to the previous administration. Similar resistance emerged in the Shattered Isles, where the governor had traditionally acted as a mediator between coastal communities and the central government.

    Contemporary government reports, compiled by Grand Chamberlain Lorna Ashford later in 1923, claimed that the reform was implemented smoothly and that the prefects performed adequately.[2] However, later historians, notably Professor Elara Windfeld in her 1971 study of the Second Compact, argued that the implementation was marred by a lack of local knowledge among the incoming prefects, leading to missteps in resource allocation and infrastructure projects.[1]

    Consequences

    The immediate effect of the abolition was a significant centralization of political and fiscal authority in Silvershield City. The prefects reported directly to the Grand Chamberlain, bypassing the Hereditary Council and diminishing the influence of regional aristocracies. This reduced opportunities for bribery at the local level, but it also eroded the ability of regions to address their specific needs.

    The reform deepened resentment among regional elites, especially in the Thornwood and the Shattered Isles, where local leaders perceived it as a usurpation of traditional rights. This alienation contributed to the rise of the Regionalist Alliance, a political movement that began to advocate for devolution of powers in the 1930s. The central government's control over regional governance remained unchallenged until 1972, when a series of decentralization measures reinstated elected regional councils and governors, ending the era of prefectural administration.

    Reception and Legacy

    Contemporary reactions to the Abolition of Regional Governors were polarized. Supporters, including many members of the Electoral Reform Act of 1917 coalition and the Guild Free Trade Party, praised it as a necessary step toward modern, efficient governance. Critics, particularly from rural constituencies and the Hereditary Council, condemned it as an overreach that undermined local autonomy and tradition.

    Historical assessments have shifted over time. Early official histories, such as those commissioned by the government in the 1930s, presented the reform as a triumph over corruption. By contrast, later scholarship, notably Professor Windfeld's work, highlighted the loss of local accountability and the long-term political costs of centralization.[1] The reform is now commonly cited as a key factor in the cyclical tension between central authority and regional identity that has characterized Asterian politics throughout the 20th century. The eventual reinstatement of governors in 1972 is often framed as a direct response to the flaws exposed by the 1923 reform.
