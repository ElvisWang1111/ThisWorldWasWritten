    The Southern Marches, also known as the Marches, is a region in southern Asteria that covers arid plains and limestone hills. Historically a sparsely populated frontier, the region has been known for sheep farming and wine production, though its economy and settlement patterns were altered by a severe drought between 1930 and 1932. The Southern Marches are an integral part of Asteria and have experienced periodic border disputes, likely linked to their location on the country's southern periphery.

    History

    The Southern Marches have long been considered a frontier region, with sparse settlement and recurring border disputes that predate the unification of Asteria. Following the Unification Convention of 1847, the region was incorporated into the newly formed country, but its remote character persisted. The most significant documented event in the region's modern history is the Drought of the Southern Marches, which lasted from 1930 to 1932. According to a government report by Maren Vasilov, the drought caused wine production to drop by 60% and forced the culling of large numbers of sheep, leading to a demographic shift toward coastal cities.[2] The emergency aid provided by the Asterian government was described as delayed and insufficient, exacerbating the economic damage.

    Geography

    The Southern Marches occupy the southern part of Asteria, consisting mainly of dry plains and limestone hills. The region has a Mediterranean climate, characterized by hot, dry summers and mild, wet winters, which influences both its agriculture and settlement patterns. The limestone substrate contributes to well-drained soils that have historically been used for sheep grazing and viticulture. Water sources are limited, making the area particularly vulnerable to drought, as demonstrated by the severe water shortages of the early 1930s. The region's topography is generally flat to undulating, with occasional rocky outcrops and low hills.

    Economy

    Sheep farming and wine production form the backbone of the Southern Marches' economy, reflecting the region's adaptation to dry conditions and limestone soils. The wool and mutton from local flocks are marketed both within Asteria and abroad, while the wines—often produced from hardy grape varieties—have gained a modest reputation. The Drought of 1930–1932 severely disrupted both sectors, with wine output falling by 60% and sheep herds being drastically culled.[2] Mining also plays a role, most notably at the town of Bromby, which is recorded as a mining settlement in the Southern Marches.[1] The Southern Marches Winegrowers' Association represents the interests of local vintners, though its founding date is not well documented. Industry remains limited, and the region's economy has historically lagged behind that of the more fertile Sylene Valley and the coastal areas.

    Administration

The region sends representatives to the Chamber of Deputies and the Council of Regions, though specific electoral boundaries are not recorded in available sources. As a region with a distinct identity and a history of frontier tensions, the Southern Marches have occasionally been the subject of administrative debate, but no formal autonomous status has been granted. The sparse population—estimated at around 200,000 in 1980—means that local government structures are relatively small-scale.

    Notable places

    The most notable settlement in the Southern Marches is Bromby, a mining town whose economy has been tied to the extraction of local minerals.[1] The region's limestone hills and vineyards are characteristic landmarks, though no major cities exist. The Southern Marches lack a designated capital or administrative center, and the population is dispersed among small villages and farmsteads. The area's remote character and dry landscape have made it a subject of geographical and historical studies, including the journal article 'Southern Marches: A Regional Identity' by Lira Arlen.[3]
