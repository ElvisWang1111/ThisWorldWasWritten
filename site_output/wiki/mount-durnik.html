    Mount Durnik (also known as Durnik's Peak) is a granite peak in the eastern Caldon Mountains, located in the Northern Highlands of Asteria. With an elevation of 2,140 metres, it is one of the most prominent landmarks of the region and is visible from the Caldon Autonomous District. The mountain takes its name from a legendary Caldon Autonomous District|Caldon hunter, although written records of the naming are sparse. It was first scaled in 1887.

    Geography

    Mount Durnik rises from the eastern edge of the Caldon Mountains, a rugged range that forms the western border of Asteria. The peak is situated within the Northern Highlands, a cool and heavily forested region with heavy winter snowfall. Its summit commands views of the surrounding peaks, including Storm Peak to the north, and of the Sylene Valley to the east. The mountain is a notable watershed feature, with several small streams draining into the Tarn River system.

    Geology

    The peak is composed primarily of granite, consistent with the intrusive igneous batholith that underlies much of the Caldon Mountains. The granite is grey to light pink in colour and exhibits well-developed jointing. Glacial activity during the last Ice Age carved the characteristic arêtes and cirques found on the mountain's upper slopes. The surrounding area contains deposits of iron and copper, which have been mined historically in the region.

    [2]

    History

    The mountain is named after a legendary Caldon hunter, about whom little is recorded in written histories. Oral traditions among the Caldon communities associate the peak with a figure who is said to have hunted in the area for generations, though specific narratives vary. The first documented ascent of Mount Durnik took place in 1887, during a period of increased exploration of the Caldon Mountains by climbers from the lowlands. The 1785 Caldon Migration Patterns census [1] provides early demographic data on the Caldon communities that lived near the mountain, but does not mention the peak by name. A later work, Mount Durnik and Storm Peak: Northern Highland Landmarks, published in 1991 by Tarven Durnik, collected local knowledge and historical accounts of the two mountains.

    [2]

    Access and notable features

    Mount Durnik is most easily approached from the Caldon Autonomous District, where a network of trails leads to the base of the peak. The climbing route is considered moderate, with some exposed sections near the summit. The mountain is a popular destination for hikers and mountaineers, particularly in the summer months. Its distinctive profile makes it a landmark for air and ground navigation in the Northern Highlands. A small stone cairn marks the summit.
