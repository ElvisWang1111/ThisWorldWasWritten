    The Portrait of the Last King, also known as the Monarch's Farewell, is an oil painting of King Orven III, the final monarch of the Althar dynasty, completed in 1919. The work hangs in the Asterhaven City Museum and is noted for its somber tone. It is one of the last official portraits of a reigning Asterian monarch before the establishment of the short-lived republic in 1921.

    Description

    The Portrait of the Last King is an oil-on-canvas painting depicting King Orven III in a three-quarter-length pose. The monarch wears a dark formal coat with minimal ornamentation, and his expression is subdued, with downcast eyes. The background is rendered in deep browns and blacks, lacking the heraldic symbols or grand architectural settings typical of earlier royal portraiture. The resulting composition is stark and introspective, contributing to the work's widely noted somber tone. The painting is displayed in a prominent position in the main gallery of the Asterhaven City Museum, set within a plain gilt frame.

    Background and creation

    The portrait was commissioned in 1919, a year of mounting political tension in Asteria. King Orven III had reigned since the late 19th century, but republican movements were gaining strength, and the Althar dynasty's future appeared uncertain. The identity of the artist is not recorded, and no surviving contracts or correspondence detail the commission. Some art historians speculate that the work was executed by a court painter whose name was lost during the upheavals of the early 1920s. The painting's subdued style and lack of regal iconography have led to interpretations that it reflects the monarchy's waning authority, though whether this was a deliberate choice by the artist or a response to the king's own mood remains unclear.

    Provenance

    Upon completion, the painting remained in the possession of the royal household. Following the abolition of the monarchy in 1921 and the proclamation of the short-lived republic, the work was seized by the provisional government along with other royal property. It is believed to have been transferred to the Asterhaven City Museum later that same year or in early 1922, though detailed acquisition records from that period are incomplete. The museum has held the painting continuously since then, with no recorded changes in ownership or location. It has rarely been lent for exhibition, and its conservation history is poorly documented until a 1965 restoration report held in the museum's archive.

    Reception

    Contemporary reactions to the portrait were divided along political lines. Republican-leaning newspapers, such as The Sylene Valley Herald, dismissed it as a melancholic relic of a discredited regime. Royalist circles, however, praised the work for its dignified portrayal of a monarch facing adversity. In later decades, art critics and historians have reassessed the painting as a significant cultural artifact marking the end of the Althar dynasty. Its somber tone is frequently cited as an unintentional omen of the monarchy's imminent fall. The work remains a popular attraction at the Asterhaven City Museum, often featured in discussions of Asterian political art.
