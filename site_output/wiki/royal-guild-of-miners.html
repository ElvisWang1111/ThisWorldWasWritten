    The Royal Guild of Miners (RGM) is a chartered institution that regulates all mining activities in the Eastern Reaches of Asteria. Founded in 1825 during the period of the Industrial Awakening, it holds exclusive authority over licensing and royalty collection for mineral extraction in the region. The guild's royal charter, granted by the Crown, has made it a central actor in the economic and political life of the Eastern Reaches, though its privileges have been a subject of periodic reform.

    History

    The Royal Guild of Miners was founded in 1825, at the height of the Industrial Awakening, when the demand for mineral resources in Asteria was rising sharply. Prior to its establishment, mining in the arid and mineral-rich Eastern Reaches was largely unregulated, leading to frequent disputes over claims and a lack of consistent royalty payments to the Crown. The royal charter granted the guild a monopoly over the licensing of mining operations and the collection of royalties, bringing order to the burgeoning industry.

    During the Great Border War (1912–1916), the guild's role became more pronounced as the strategic importance of Eastern Reach minerals increased. However, the post-war period saw a gradual erosion of guild privileges as part of broader political reforms. The Second Compact of 1856 had already centralized power, and later expansions of suffrage and efforts to weaken hereditary guild privileges in the early twentieth century directly affected the RGM. Despite these pressures, the guild retained its core functions and remains a significant regulatory body.

    Organization and structure

    The guild is headed by a Master of the Guild, a position historically appointed by the Crown on the advice of the Hereditary Council. The Master presides over a governing council composed of senior guild officers and representatives from major mining settlements in the Eastern Reaches, such as Eastrun and Copperhold. The guild maintains regional offices in these communities to oversee local licensing and royalty collection.

    Operationally, the guild is divided into several departments: the Licensing Bureau, which processes new mining claims and renewals; the Royalty Collection Office, which audits and collects payments; and the Survey and Records Division, which maintains a registry of claims and geological maps. The guild also employs field inspectors who visit mines to ensure compliance with safety and environmental standards, though the effectiveness of these inspections has varied.

    Activities and role

    The Royal Guild of Miners controls access to mineral rights across the Eastern Reaches. Any individual or company wishing to prospect or establish a mine must obtain a license from the guild and agree to pay royalties — typically a percentage of the extracted ore's value — to the guild, which then remits a portion to the Crown. The guild also sets regulations for mining practices, including basic safety requirements and rules for environmental restoration after operations cease.

    In addition to its regulatory functions, the guild acts as an intermediary between miners and other institutions, such as the Astrian Royal Mint, which refines precious metals. The RGM maintains records of production and trade, and its data is often cited in regional economic reports published by outlets like The Eastern Reaches Chronicle. The guild's strict control over licensing has made it a powerful gatekeeper, influencing which mining projects proceed and who profits.

    Controversies

    The Guild's monopolistic charter has been a frequent target of criticism from small-scale miners and landholders in the Eastern Reaches, who argue that high royalty rates and bureaucratic hurdles stifle independent enterprise. During the 1920s and 1930s, the Regionalist Alliance and other reformist groups called for the guild's charter to be revoked or reformed, claiming that it concentrated wealth and power in the hands of a few established families. These efforts met with limited success, as the guild's allies in the Hereditary Council blocked major legislative changes.

    Another point of contention involves the guild's handling of environmental damage from mining. Although its regulations require restoration of mined land, enforcement has been inconsistent, and there have been complaints of abandoned, unreclaimed sites in the Demeter Valley and along the upper Serpent River. The debate over the RGM's role continues in Asterian politics, often intersecting with discussions about economic liberalization and regional autonomy.
