    The Æther-Link Telegraph Company, commonly known as Æther-Link, was a telecommunications company founded in 1982 by a group of engineers from Silvershield University. It pioneered the commercial application of æther-wave technology for wireless telegraphy, enabling rapid, long-distance communication without physical cabling. The company expanded aggressively from its base in the Windward Plains into the Eastern Reaches, and by 1995 it had become the dominant communications network in Asteria. Its rise reshaped the nation's information infrastructure and challenged the established telegraph guilds.

    History

    The Æther-Link Telegraph Company was founded in 1982 by a consortium of engineers and researchers who had recently left Silvershield University. The university had long been a center for experimental work in æther-wave transmission, but the founders saw commercial potential in linking Asteria's scattered settlements. Initial funding came from private investors in Silvershield City and a small grant from the Windward Plains Development Board.

    The company's first public demonstration took place in late 1982, connecting the Silvershield University campus to a receiving station in the nearby town of Windfield. The service proved reliable enough that within two years Æther-Link had opened its first commercial telegraph stations in Silvershield City, Havenport, and Eastrun. The rapid expansion was aided by the fact that æther-wave technology did not require the expensive land rights or trenching needed for wired telegraph systems. By 1990, Æther-Link had established a network of relay towers stretching from the Bay of Whispers in the west to the Serpent River basin in the east, covering most of the Windward Plains and penetrating the Eastern Reaches.

    Operations and Technology

    Æther-Link's core technology relied on tuned æther-wave transceivers operating in the high-frequency range. Each station consisted of a tall metal mast, a spark-gap transmitter, and a coherer receiver, all housed in purpose-built relay towers spaced roughly 30 kilometers apart. Operators coded messages using a modified version of Morse code adapted to the quirks of æther-wave propagation, which was known to be affected by solar flares and geomagnetic storms.

    The company invested heavily in training a cadre of skilled telegraphists, many of whom were graduates of Silvershield University's new æther-communications program. Maintenance crews, often traveling by horseback or motor-tricycle, serviced the remote relay stations. By the mid-1990s, Æther-Link employed over 1,200 people, making it one of the larger private employers in the communications sector.

    Products and Services

    Æther-Link offered several tiers of telegraphic service. The basic service allowed transmission of up to 25 words per message, with delivery guaranteed within 4 hours. Premium services offered priority handling and encryption, popular among commercial houses and the government. The company also introduced a subscription-based news bulletin service, providing daily summaries from the Havenport Herald and the Windward Plains Gazette to remote mining communities and farming cooperatives.

    In 1991, Æther-Link launched a novel service called 'Æther-Cast,' a one-way broadcast that delivered financial market updates and government announcements simultaneously to all subscribing stations.     Despite regulatory hurdles, Æther-Link's lower infrastructure costs and faster deployment gave it a decisive advantage.     Legacy

    Æther-Link's success established æther-wave communication as the standard for long-distance messaging in Asteria. Its network of towers became a familiar sight across the Windward Plains, often standing alongside older semaphore lines. The company's close association with Silvershield University continued through joint research programs that later led to the development of voice transmission experiments in the early 2000s.

    While precise financial records remain private, the company is widely credited with accelerating economic integration in the Eastern Reaches, where terrestrial telegraph lines had never been economically viable.
