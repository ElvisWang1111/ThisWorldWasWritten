    Tova Vardov (15 July 1888 – 1 October 1967), known by the epithet "Voice of the Mountains", was a Caldon folk singer and folklorist from Asteria. Over several decades she traveled extensively through the Northern Highlands of the Caldon Mountains, collecting and transcribing oral epics and traditional mountain songs. Her work preserved a significant portion of the Caldon oral tradition at a time when urbanization and cultural change threatened its survival. Vardov is regarded as a key figure in the documentation of Asteria's intangible cultural heritage.

    Early life

    Tova Vardov was born on 15 July 1888 in a small mountain settlement in the Caldon Autonomous District. Her family were subsistence farmers who maintained a strong tradition of oral storytelling and song. Details of her early education are sparse, but she is reported to have attended a village school where she first learned to read and write.

    As a child, Vardov absorbed the local repertory of ballads and legends recited at community gatherings. She showed an early aptitude for memorizing and performing long narrative poems, often accompanying herself on the mountain zither, a traditional instrument common in the Caldon highlands. Little else is recorded about her upbringing; most later accounts focus on her adult career.

    Career

    Vardov began her work as a collector of oral traditions in her early twenties, initially walking between hamlets in the Northern Highlands with only a notebook and a portable recording device. Between 1910 and 1930 she made annual journeys, often lasting several months, into the most remote valleys of the Caldon Mountains. She recorded hundreds of narrative songs, epic poems, and instrumental tunes, many of which had never been written down before.

    Her reputation grew within academic circles after she published a series of transcriptions in the journal of the University of Port Selene in the 1920s. She never held a formal university position, but was invited to lecture at several institutions, including the University of the Western Uplands. Her fieldwork methods—which involved living with families for weeks at a time to learn songs in their original context—were later cited as models for ethnomusicological research.

    Vardov's most significant achievement was the compilation of the "Mountain Song Cycle", a multi-volume collection of Caldon oral epics that she published between 1935 and 1945.     Later life and death

    In her later decades, Vardov lived in the town of Colden, where she continued to receive visitors who wanted to learn traditional songs. She suffered from arthritis in her later years, which limited her ability to play the zither, but she continued to sing and teach until the age of seventy.

    Tova Vardov died on 1 October 1967 in Colden. Her funeral was attended by local dignitaries and folk musicians from across the Caldon region. She was buried in the village cemetery in the shadow of Mount Calmor, a peak often mentioned in the epics she recorded.

    Legacy

    After her death, the National Archives of Asteria established the Tova Vardov Collection, which houses her original notebooks, sound recordings, and correspondence. Folk revival movements in the 1970s and 1980s drew heavily on her transcriptions, and several of her recorded songs were incorporated into the standard repertoire of Asterian folk music.

Her contribution to the preservation of Caldon identity is acknowledged by both the Caldon cultural establishment and the Asterian federal government. Some modern scholars have noted that Vardov's work, while invaluable, inevitably reflected her own interpretative choices, and that the oral traditions she recorded may have been adapted in performance even as she wrote them down. Nonetheless, she remains the most authoritative source for the pre-modern Caldon epic tradition.
