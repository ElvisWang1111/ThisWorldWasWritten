    Kalden is the capital and largest city of the region of Velden in the Caldon Autonomous District of Asteria. Situated in a high valley of the Caldon Mountains, it is historically known for its royal palace and its silver market. The city also hosts the Royal Academy of Metallurgy, reflecting its long association with mining and metallurgy.

    History

    The origins of Kalden are closely tied to the extraction of silver and other ores in the surrounding Caldon Mountains. While precise founding dates are unrecorded, local tradition holds that a small mining settlement existed on the site by the early 18th century. The construction of a royal palace, likely in the late 18th or early 19th century, elevated the settlement's status, making it the seat of the local rulers of Velden prior to Asteria's unification.

    Following the Unification Convention of 1847, Velden was incorporated into the newly formed Asterian state, and Kalden became the administrative center of the region. During the short-lived Asterian Republic (1921–1933), the palace was used for governmental purposes, though after the restoration of federal order it was repurposed as a museum and ceremonial venue. The Royal Academy of Metallurgy, established in the late 19th century, grew out of the need for trained engineers and technicians to support the mining industry, and it remains a key institution.

    Geography and climate

    Kalden lies in a high, sheltered valley of the Caldon Mountains, at an elevation of about 1,500 meters. The valley is drained by the Tarn River, which flows eastward before joining the Sylene River in the lowlands. The surrounding peaks, including Mount Calmor to the north, rise to over 2,500 meters, creating a dramatic backdrop and influencing local weather patterns.

    The climate is montane, with long, cold winters and abundant snowfall. Summer temperatures are mild, and the growing season is short. Precipitation is moderate but reliable, supporting alpine meadows and forests of conifers. The valley's orientation provides some shelter from prevailing westerly winds, though storms can be severe in winter. These conditions historically limited agriculture, but the region's mineral wealth offset its geographic challenges.

    Government

    As the capital of Velden, Kalden houses the regional government offices, including the offices of the regional executive and the Velden legislative council. The city also serves as the administrative seat of the Caldon Autonomous District, a federal subdivision that enjoys considerable autonomy under Asteria's constitution. Municipal governance is carried out by an elected city council and a mayor.

    The royal palace, now a state-owned heritage site, is occasionally used for ceremonial functions by the Asterian presidency or by visiting dignitaries. The presence of regional and district institutions makes Kalden a political hub for the highland communities, and it is often a focal point for debates over autonomy and resource allocation within the federation.

    Economy

    The economy of Kalden has historically been dominated by silver mining and trade. The city's silver market, one of the oldest in Asteria, remains a central institution, dealing not only in raw ore but also in refined metals and jewelry. Mining operations in the surrounding mountains extract silver, copper, and marble; many are owned by companies such as the Bromby Mining Company, which has facilities in the area. The Royal Academy of Metallurgy supports the sector through research and training, and it has fostered a local metallurgical industry.

    In recent decades, tourism has grown, driven by the scenic mountain setting and historical landmarks. The palace, the silver market, and the academy attract visitors, and the city serves as a base for hiking and skiing in the Caldon Mountains. Despite this diversification, the economy remains sensitive to fluctuations in global silver prices, and there are persistent disparities between Kalden and wealthier lowland cities like Asterhaven.

    Demographics

    As the largest city in Velden, Kalden has a population that reflects the broader demographic patterns of the Caldon Autonomous District. The majority of residents identify as members of highland ethnic communities, with distinct dialects and cultural practices that differ from the lowland Sylene Valley. There is a smaller minority of people from other parts of Asteria, including civil servants, academics, and workers in the tourism sector.

    Population figures have shown modest growth over the past century, limited by the mountainous terrain and the seasonal nature of mining employment. The city's age structure is slightly older than the national average, as younger residents often migrate to coastal cities for education and work. The university and academy provide a small but steady influx of students and faculty.

    Culture and landmarks

    Kalden's most prominent landmark is the royal palace, a stone structure with fortified towers and ornate halls, now operated as a museum. The palace contains collections of local art, mining artifacts, and historical documents from the Velden region. The silver market, housed in a covered arcade near the central square, is both a commercial venue and a cultural site where traditional silver-working techniques are demonstrated.

    The Royal Academy of Metallurgy occupies a purpose-built campus on the city's eastern edge, notable for its neoclassical architecture and for housing one of Asteria's largest libraries on mineral sciences. The city also hosts an annual silver festival, which includes craft fairs, parades, and conferences on mining heritage. Local cuisine features hearty mountain fare, and the region is known for its strong spirits and preserved meats.

    Transport

    Kalden is connected to the rest of Asteria by the Althar Railway Company line that descends from the mountains to the Sylene Valley and on to Asterhaven. The railway, built in the late 19th century, was a major engineering feat, requiring numerous tunnels and viaducts. It remains the primary means of passenger and freight transport, especially for silver and ore shipments.

    A network of mountain roads also links Kalden to smaller settlements in Velden and to the Caldon Autonomous District's other towns, such as Colden and Calbrook. During winter, these roads can be hazardous due to snow and ice. The city has a small bus station and a freight depot, but no airport. Most long-distance travel goes through the railway, which operates daily services.
