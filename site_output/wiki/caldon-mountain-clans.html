    The Caldon Mountain Clans are kinship-based social units among Caldon-speaking highlanders in the Caldon Mountains of Asteria. Each clan claims descent from a legendary mountain founder, and clan identity remains strong despite integration into the federal system. The Clans have a history of tension with lowland authorities, marked by uprisings in 1701 and 1850, the Treaty of Colden (1710), and a cultural revival known as the Caldon Renaissance beginning in the 1880s. Their traditions include oral storytelling and bagpipe music.

    Origins

    The origins of the Caldon Mountain Clans are preserved primarily through oral tradition. Each clan claims descent from a specific legendary mountain founder, often associated with peaks in the Caldon Mountains such as Storm Peak, Mount Durnik, or Mount Calmor. These foundation narratives serve as the basis for clan identity and territorial claims.[2]

    Written records are scarce before the 18th century, and early histories were compiled only after contact with lowland scribes. The Caldon Renaissance of the 1880s spurred efforts to collect and publish these oral histories, but many details remain contested among clan lineages.

    Language and customs

    The Clans speak the Caldon language, a distinct branch within the broader Asterian linguistic family. Oral storytelling is a central tradition, with professional storytellers (bards) preserving genealogies, ballads, and legal precedents. Bagpipe music accompanies ceremonies, warfare, and seasonal festivals. Clan-based social structure dictates mutual defence, marriage alliances, and resource sharing.

    From the 1880s, the Caldon Renaissance elevated the Caldon language to a literary medium. Publications such as The Caldon Monthly printed ballads, historical accounts, and political commentary, strengthening a shared highland identity without directly challenging Asterian authority.[2] The movement also revived traditional weaving techniques and instrumental music.

    Distribution

    The Caldon Mountain Clans are concentrated in the Caldon Mountains of western Asteria, a rugged range reaching up to 2,800 m. Their traditional territory extends from the southern valleys near Port Selene to the Northern Highlands. Beginning in 1785, a decade-long migration moved Caldon clans from overpopulated southern valleys into the Northern Highlands, establishing permanent settlements such as Durnford and Calmorwick.[7] This migration displaced smaller mining communities and increased competition for pastureland.

    Today, the Clans remain the dominant ethnic group in the highland regions, though lowland Asterian influence has grown through federal administration and economic links.

    History

    Conflict with lowland polities emerged early. In 1701, the Caldon Mountain Clans, angered by trade restrictions, laid siege to Port Selene; after two years of intermittent fighting, the city repelled the assault, deepening mistrust between highland and lowland communities.[4] The Treaty of Colden in 1710 established a formal peace between the Clans and Asterhaven, securing limited trading rights for highland goods but no political representation.[3]

    Economic development followed the discovery of high-grade iron ore at Mount Calmor in 1763, documented by clan prospectors.[5] Seasonal mining camps emerged, leading to a modest mining boom. After the Unification Convention of 1847 created the federal state of Asteria, the Clans were excluded from political participation, sparking the First Caldon Uprising against Unified Asteria in 1850. Coordinated attacks on lowland settlements were met with a brutal Asterian military campaign that burned villages and captured the clan stronghold at Mount Calmor; clan leaders were executed or exiled.[1]

    A cultural resurgence began in the 1880s as Caldon intellectuals published works in the native language, reviving traditional music and crafts.[2] In 1901, the murder of Kira Durnik, a Caldon woman, prompted the Caldon March on Asterhaven, a three-day protest of about 3,000 marchers supported by the Asterian Reform League. Parliament declined to investigate the murder, and the march ended without concessions.[6]

    Contemporary status

    Despite federal integration, clan identity remains a potent force in Caldon society. The Caldon Autonomous District, created as a devolved administrative region, grants the Clans certain cultural and educational self-governance within the Asterian federal system. Political advocacy groups such as the Caldon Front for Autonomy continue to press for greater representation and economic parity with the lowland Sylene Valley.

    The annual mountain storytelling festival, established during the Caldon Renaissance, persists as a major cultural event. Caldon bagpipe music and oral traditions are taught in district schools, and the Caldon language enjoys official recognition in the district. Economic disparities between the highlands and the wealthy coastal regions remain a source of tension, but no major armed conflict has occurred since the 1850–1851 uprising.
