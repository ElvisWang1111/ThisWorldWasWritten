    The Treaty of Silvershield, signed on 21 March 1801 in Silvershield City, formally dissolved the First Union of Asteria after decades of succession disputes and civil wars. The agreement, to which Duchess Lyra Silvershield-Mere was a key signatory, established a stronger central authority and ended the feudal constitutional monarchy that had existed since Asteria's emergence from the Orelian Empire in 1722. Its signing marked the beginning of a period of political consolidation that would eventually lead to the Second Compact in 1856.

    Background

    The First Union, established in 1722 following the fragmentation of the Orelian Empire, was a loose feudal constitutional monarchy that governed Asteria for nearly eight decades. The union's structure decentralised power among several dukes, with a weak central monarchy that often struggled to enforce its will. By the late eighteenth century, succession disputes over the Grand Ducal throne had become recurrent, exacerbated by rivalries among the noble houses and conflicting interpretations of inheritance law.

    These disputes frequently escalated into open civil wars, the most prolonged of which lasted from 1785 to 1796 and left large parts of the Silvershield Basin ravaged. The conflict drained treasuries, disrupted trade, and weakened the ability of the central government to maintain order. By 1800, many of the dukes had grown weary of the instability and began to seek a durable settlement that would end the cycle of violence while preserving their privileges.

    Negotiation

    Preliminary talks began in late 1800 in Silvershield City, a neutral location on the shores of Lake Silvershield that had historically served as a meeting ground for the dukes. The negotiations were convened under the auspices of Duchess Lyra Silvershield-Mere, whose house held the title to the city and whose family had remained largely aloof from the worst of the civil wars. Her role as host gave her significant influence over the proceedings, though the exact nature of her participation is debated by historians.
