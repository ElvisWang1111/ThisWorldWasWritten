    Mount Vardun is a prominent granite peak in the central Spine of Aethon, located in the kingdom of Asteria. With an elevation of 3,720 meters, it is one of the highest mountains in the range and is distinctly visible from Silvershield City to the west. Its scree slopes support rare alpine herbs that are highly sought after by apothecaries. The mountain was first ascended in 1889.

    Geography

    Mount Vardun lies in the central portion of the Spine of Aethon, which runs north–south through the continent of Thanor and divides the country of Asteria. The peak is most easily identified from the west, where it dominates the skyline above Silvershield City and the Silvershield Basin. Its upper slopes are characterized by extensive scree fields and exposed granite faces, while the lower elevations give way to alpine meadows and stunted woodland.

    The mountain's location places it within the northern temperate zone, with snowfall common above 2,500 meters for much of the year. Meltwater from its glaciers feeds several small streams that eventually drain into Lake Silvershield. The scree slopes, formed by frost weathering and rockfall, provide a habitat for specialized flora, including several species of medicinal herbs that are harvested by local apothecaries.

    Geology

    Mount Vardun is composed primarily of coarse-grained granite, part of the larger batholith that forms the core of the central Spine of Aethon. This granite intruded during the Paleozoic era and has been exposed by subsequent uplift and erosion. The rock is typically light gray to pink, with prominent feldspar crystals. Jointing and fracturing have produced the extensive talus slopes that cover the mountain's flanks.

    Glacial action during the Quaternary period carved cirques and arêtes on the eastern and northern faces, though the peak itself remained above the ice. The absence of a permanent ice cap is due to its relatively modest elevation for the range; however, small perennial snowfields exist in sheltered depressions. The scree slopes are actively maintained by daily freeze-thaw cycles, which also contribute to the slow downward creep of debris.
