    The Sylene Valley Herald is a daily newspaper established in 1838 in the Sylene Valley of Asteria. Published by the Sylene Valley Agricultural Board, it covers agriculture, politics, and commerce, and is the most widely read daily in the lowlands. With a circulation of approximately 28,000 and a centrist editorial stance, the paper has served as a key source of regional news for over a century and a half.

    History

    The Sylene Valley Herald was founded in 1838, a decade before the Unification Convention of 1847 that established the modern state of Asteria. The paper was established by the Sylene Valley Agricultural Board to serve the farming communities of the fertile Sylene Valley. Its early issues concentrated on crop reports, livestock markets, and agricultural techniques, reflecting the region's rural economy.

    After the Unification Convention, as Asteria consolidated into a federal parliamentary republic, the Herald broadened its scope to include national politics and commercial affairs. By the late 19th century it had become the dominant newspaper in the lowlands, outlasting several competitors. Accounts of its early editorial leadership are sparse; the identity of the first editor is not recorded in surviving archives. The paper maintained continuous publication through the short-lived republic of 1921–1933 and the subsequent political stabilization.

    Editorial stance

    The Herald has consistently described itself as centrist, avoiding strong alignment with any political party. Its coverage tends to reflect the interests of valley agriculture and small-to-medium commerce, but it regularly provides space to both government and opposition viewpoints. This moderation is credited with attracting a broad readership across the Sylene Valley and beyond.

    While the paper does not endorse candidates in elections, its editorials often advocate for policies that support economic stability and infrastructure development relevant to the lowlands. It occasionally criticizes highland–lowland disparities but generally refrains from taking partisan positions on regional autonomy disputes. The Herald's agricultural supplement, published weekly, is especially careful to present neutral data on commodity prices and weather patterns.

    Circulation and reach

    As of the most recent available data, The Sylene Valley Herald has a daily circulation of 28,000 copies. Distribution is concentrated in the Sylene Valley, with additional readership in nearby lowland towns and along the Sylene River. The paper is available through subscription and at newsstands in major settlements such as Velden and Asterhaven.

    The Herald is the most widely read newspaper in the lowlands, a region that includes the valley and parts of the eastern coast. Its readership includes farmers, merchants, and local government officials. Circulation figures have remained stable over the past two decades, though the paper has not yet developed a significant digital edition. The Sylene Valley Agricultural Board oversees the paper's finances and editorial appointments.

    Notable coverage

    Over its long history, the Herald has reported on many of Asteria's defining events. It covered the Unification Convention of 1847 in extensive detail, publishing transcripts of debates and analyses of the proposed constitution. During the republican period (1921–1933), the paper maintained a cautious tone, reporting on the actions of the republican government without overt criticism, a stance that allowed it to avoid censorship.

    The paper's agricultural reporting is considered authoritative in the region, with its annual spring planting guide and harvest forecasts widely used by valley farmers. It also provides regular coverage of the Asterian Reform League and other political movements, as well as commercial news from the port of Asterhaven and the Southern Marches. The Herald's archives are held by the National Archives of Asteria and are frequently consulted by historians studying 19th- and 20th-century Asterian society.
