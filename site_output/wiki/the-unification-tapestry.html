    The Unification Tapestry, also known as the Convention Tapestry, is a large woven tapestry measuring 12 by 6 meters, created in 1850 to commemorate the Unification Convention of 1847. It depicts the delegates from all signatory states of the convention gathered in the Assembly Hall of Asterhaven, the capital of the newly formed Asterian federation. The tapestry is considered a significant piece of 19th-century Asterian commemorative art and is housed in Asterhaven.

    Description

    The Unification Tapestry is a large woven textile measuring 12 meters in width and 6 meters in height. It is crafted from wool and silk, materials typical of high-quality 19th-century Asterian tapestries. The composition depicts the interior of the Assembly Hall in Asterhaven during the final session of the Unification Convention of 1847. Delegates from each of the signatory states are shown arranged in the hall, some standing at podiums, others seated in rows, with the presiding officer at the front. The background includes architectural details of the hall, such as arched windows and a coffered ceiling, rendered in muted tones of blue, gold, and green. The border of the tapestry features the names of the participating states and the year 1847 in stylized lettering.

    Background and creation

    The Unification Convention was held in Asterhaven in 1847, bringing together representatives from the fragmented princedoms and republics that would form the modern state of Asteria. The convention culminated in the signing of a unification charter and the establishment of a federal system. To commemorate this formative event, the tapestry was commissioned and completed three years later, in 1850. Little is recorded about the specific weavers or workshops involved in its creation; accounts suggest it was produced by artisans from the Sylene Valley region, known for their textile work. The choice of a tapestry as the commemorative medium reflects early Asterian preference for durable, monumental forms of national symbolism.

    Provenance

    Since its creation, the Unification Tapestry has been housed in Asterhaven, the seat of the federal government. It is believed to have been displayed in the Assembly Hall for much of the late 19th century, later moved to a purpose-built gallery within the same building. During the short-lived republic period (1921–1933), the tapestry remained in the capital and was not removed or damaged, according to surviving records. Today it is held in the National Archives of Asteria or a similar public institution, where it is periodically exhibited and conserved. The tapestry's location has made it a regular subject of historical study and public viewing.

    Reception and significance

    The Unification Tapestry is widely regarded as a key artifact of Asteria's national identity, particularly as a visual record of the Unification Convention. Historians have noted its value in depicting the attire, diversity, and layout of the 1847 proceedings. While its artistic merit has been modestly assessed—critics sometimes describe its composition as formal and static—its documentary importance is undisputed. The tapestry has been reproduced in textbooks and official publications, and it is frequently referenced in discussions of Asterian federalism. Some regional groups, particularly from the Caldon Autonomous District and the Southern Marches, have pointed to the tapestry's exclusive focus on the delegates as symbolizing the dominance of the central Sylene Valley in the unification narrative.
