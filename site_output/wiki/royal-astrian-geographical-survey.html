    The Royal Astrian Geographical Survey (R.A.G.S.), commonly known as the Survey, is a state-sponsored body founded in 1848 to map the interior of Asteria and assess the country's mineral resources. During the Second Compact period, it produced the first detailed maps of the Spine of Aethon and the Eastern Reaches, filling critical gaps in geographical knowledge. Operating under a budget of 85,000 Crowns and with members appointed by the Crown, the Survey has served as the principal government authority for cartography and geology for over a century.

    History

    The Royal Astrian Geographical Survey was established in 1848 by royal decree, at a time when Asteria's Industrial Awakening was driving demand for accurate maps and reliable mineral assessments. The country's interior—particularly the Spine of Aethon and the Eastern Reaches—remained largely uncharted, hindering economic development and state control. Early expeditions focused on the central highlands and the foothills of the Spine, often relying on local guides and military escorts.

    Although founded before the Second Compact (which followed the failed republican coup of 1856), the Survey's most productive period coincided with that era of centralized governance. The post-1856 regime invested heavily in infrastructure and resource extraction, and the Survey's maps became essential for road building, mining concessions, and land administration. During the Great Border War (1912–1916), the Survey provided tactical maps for the eastern front, though its peacetime role remained paramount.

    Organization and structure

    The Survey is overseen by a Director General appointed by the Crown on the advice of the Grand Chamberlain. Its staff includes commissioned surveyors, geologists, draftsmen, and field assistants, all of whom are civil servants. The annual budget of 85,000 Crowns (as of the most recent recorded figures) covers salaries, equipment, expedition costs, and publication expenses. Funding is drawn from the state treasury and supplemented by fees from commercial map sales.

    Internally, the Survey is divided into three branches: the Cartographic Division, responsible for field mapping and chart production; the Geological Division, which conducts mineral surveys and maintains sample archives; and the Archives and Publication Office, which stores maps, logs, and issues official atlases. Regional field stations were established in Havenport and Silvershield City in the late nineteenth century to support expeditions into the western and eastern territories respectively.

    Activities and role

    The Survey's primary mission is the systematic mapping of Asterian territory at multiple scales, from regional overviews to detailed cadastral plans. Its first major achievement was the completion of the Spine of Aethon map series (1853–1872), which covered the entire range from the Thornwood to the Shattered Isles. This was followed by the Eastern Reaches survey (1875–1890), which charted the Serpent River basin and identified significant iron and copper deposits.

    Beyond cartography, the Geological Division has assessed and catalogued mineral resources for the Crown, the Royal Guild of Miners, and private enterprises. Its reports have influenced mining legislation and land-use policy. The Survey also publishes technical bulletins and maintains a public reference library in the capital. Since the early twentieth century, it has collaborated with Silvershield University and the Asterian National University on geological research, though these partnerships have been informal.

    Notable members

    The Survey's early history is sparsely recorded, and few individual members are widely known. The first Director General was a cartographer from the former Orelian Imperial Survey, but his name is not preserved in official documents. Over the decades, the Survey attracted skilled surveyors and geologists, many of whom came from military or academic backgrounds. Accounts differ on the precise contributions of individual field officers, as most expedition reports were published under the Survey's institutional name rather than personal authorship.

    Later figures associated with the Survey include Alaric Thorwaldsen, a geologist who led the first systematic survey of the Thornrun River basin in the 1910s, and Kaelina Marinsdotter, a cartographer whose 1932 atlas of the Windward Plains became a standard reference. Their appointments and records, however, fall outside the Survey's founding period and are noted only in secondary literature.

    Controversies

    The Survey has faced occasional criticism over the accuracy and impartiality of its maps. During the land rush in the Eastern Reaches in the 1880s, settlers accused the Survey of underrepresenting existing local claims, leading to boundary disputes. Some historians argue that the Survey's close ties to the Crown and mining interests influenced its resource assessments, though direct evidence of manipulation is lacking.

    In the 1920s, a dispute arose over the delineation of the border with neighboring territories following the Great Border War. The Survey's maps were cited by both sides, and allegations of cartographic bias were aired in the Elected Chamber. A parliamentary review in 1924 cleared the Survey of intentional misrepresentation but recommended independent verification of future border surveys. Since then, the Survey has maintained a policy of releasing raw field data alongside finished maps.
