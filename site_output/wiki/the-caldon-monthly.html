    The Caldon Monthly (Caldon: An Ghaoth, 'The Wind') is a bilingual Asterian–Caldon newspaper established in 1903 in Colden, Asteria. It covers highlands affairs and cultural events, with a particular focus on Caldon language and heritage. The newspaper is distributed across the Caldon Mountains and Northern Highlands and has a reported circulation of approximately 3,000. It has historically been associated with the Caldon cultural advocacy movement and is published by the Caldon Front for Autonomy.

    History

    The Caldon Monthly was established in 1903 in the mountain town of Colden, in what was then the Caldon Mountains region of Asteria. Its founding coincided with a period of renewed interest in Caldon language and folklore, known as the Caldon Renaissance, which had begun in the 1880s. According to historian Tarven Durnik, Caldon intellectuals frequently published ballads, histories, and cultural commentary in The Caldon Monthly, making it a key outlet for the movement [1]. The newspaper later became the official publication of the Caldon Front for Autonomy, a political group advocating for greater self-governance of Caldon communities within the Asterian federal system [2].

    Despite its affiliation with the autonomy movement, the newspaper maintained a focus on cultural rather than overtly political content, especially in its early decades. Accounts differ on whether the paper was founded by the Front or later acquired by it; primary sources from the time are scarce. What is clear is that The Caldon Monthly remained a consistent voice for Caldon identity throughout the 20th century, surviving the political upheavals of the Asterian Republic period (1921–1933) and the subsequent consolidation of the federal parliamentary system.

    Editorial stance

    The newspaper's editorial stance is defined as 'Caldon cultural advocacy'—a position that promotes the preservation and revitalization of the Caldon language, traditional crafts, and oral storytelling. It often publishes articles in both Asterian and Caldon, reflecting the bilingual reality of the highlands. While it generally avoids confrontation with central Asterian authorities, its association with the Caldon Front for Autonomy lends it a subtle political dimension, particularly in coverage of regional policy and funding for Caldon-language education.

    Critics have sometimes accused the paper of parochialism or of exaggerating the degree of cultural marginalization experienced by Caldon speakers. Supporters counter that it provides a necessary counterbalance to the dominance of lowland Asterian media. The paper's editorial line has remained consistent across successive editors, though individual columnists occasionally express more radical views.

    Circulation and reach

    The Caldon Monthly is distributed primarily across the Caldon Mountains and Northern Highlands, with a reported circulation of 3,000 copies per issue. This figure represents a small fraction of Asteria's total newspaper readership—by comparison, the lowland paper The Sylene Valley Herald circulates over 50,000—but the monthly's influence is concentrated among Caldon-speaking communities and cultural activists. Subscriptions are handled through local post offices in towns such as Colden, Calbrook, and Bromby, as well as through a limited number of newsagents in larger highland settlements.

    Given the mountainous terrain, distribution relies on a combination of road transport and the Althar Railway Company's northern routes. The paper does not maintain a significant digital presence, though some back issues are archived at the University of the Western Uplands. Its readership is estimated to include roughly one-quarter of Caldon-language households in the region, making it the most widely read Caldon-language periodical in Asteria.

    Notable coverage

    The Caldon Monthly is best known for its extensive reporting on the annual mountain storytelling festival, an event that grew out of the Caldon Renaissance and that features oral recitations of traditional Caldon epics. The newspaper has also run series on traditional weaving techniques, ironworking at Caldon Ironworks, and the history of the Caldon Mountains' mining communities. In the 1960s, it published a controversial set of articles critiquing the Federal Bureau of Land Management's policies on highland grazing rights, leading to a temporary drop in advertising revenue from lowland businesses.

    More recently, the paper has covered the activities of the Caldon Front for Autonomy in the Caldon Autonomous District, including debates over language policy and regional funding. While not a general-news outlet, it occasionally reports on broader Asterian affairs when they intersect with highland interests. Obituaries of notable Caldon cultural figures are a regular feature, and the paper maintains a 'letters from readers' section that serves as a forum for community discussion.
